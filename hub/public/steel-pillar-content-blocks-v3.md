### 3Cr13

**Tags:** `Budget` | `Martensitic Stainless` | `Kitchen Cutlery` | `Mass Production` | `Wholesale-Friendly`

**Overview:**
3Cr13 is a basic martensitic stainless steel in the Chinese GB standard system, equivalent to AISI 420 and closely comparable to 420J2 in the Western market. Its low carbon content (~0.3%) is intentional — not a flaw — delivering maximum toughness and corrosion resistance at the lowest possible production cost. As the most widely produced entry-level stainless steel in China's knife industry, it serves as the de facto standard for promotional cutlery, gift knife sets, and high-volume retail programs in Western mass-market channels.

### 154CM / CPM-154

**Tags:** `Mid-High` | `Martensitic Stainless` | `EDC Knives` | `Chef Knives` | `American Standard`

**Overview:**
154CM was developed by Crucible Industries in the 1970s as an upgraded version of 440C, with molybdenum added (~0.4% Mo) to improve toughness and refine carbide distribution. It became the defining American high-end stainless of the 1980s–1990s, used by custom makers and production brands alike. CPM-154 is the powder-metallurgy reissue of the same chemistry, produced via Crucible's CPM process to eliminate carbide segregation and improve batch-to-batch consistency. In practical terms, 154CM and CPM-154 offer VG-10-class edge retention with American sourcing, while CPM-154 adds measurably better toughness and grindability. Comparable to ATS-34 (nearly identical chemistry) and positioned just below S30V in the performance ladder.

**Overview:**

It is widely specified by military-surplus and bushcraft knife makers (Ontario, Cold Steel, Condor) for machetes, survival knives, and camp blades. In the Chinese domestic system, T10 is the closest equivalent. Comparable to European C75 in chemistry and intent.

The trade-off is lower edge retention than 1095 or O1, but for large choppers, survival blades, and swords where impact survival matters more than shaving sharpness, 5160 is the default choice. It is comparable in toughness intent to 1075, but with added chromium for slightly better hardenability and corrosion resistance. Favored by bladesmiths forging big knives.

### Pattern-Welded Damascus

**Tags:** `Artisan` | `Layered Steel` | `Custom Knives` | `Collector` | `Aesthetic-Focused`

**Overview:**
Pattern-welded Damascus is a traditional forging technique in which two or more dissimilar steels (typically a high-carbon steel such as 1095 or 1084, and a nickel-alloy steel such as 15N20) are stacked, forge-welded, folded, and manipulated to create a billet with hundreds or thousands of alternating layers. Acid etching reveals the pattern, producing the iconic flowing-water or ladder-like visual effect. The blade's cutting performance is determined almost entirely by the core or edge steel used; the Damascus layering is primarily aesthetic, though the lamination can impart slight toughness benefits. Modern pattern-welded Damascus ranges from hand-forged artisan billets to machine-laminated production Damascus. It is the most widely recognized "premium" visual feature in the custom and mid-tier knife market.

### San Mai (三枚合)

**Tags:** `Traditional` | `Layered Steel` | `Japanese Chef Knives` | `Premium`

**Overview:**
San Mai (literally "three pieces") is a Japanese laminated-blade construction in which a hard, high-performance steel core is forge-welded between two layers of softer, tougher iron or stainless steel. The core steel (hagane) provides the cutting edge — typically VG-10, SG2, Shirogami, or Aogami — while the outer cladding (jigane) protects against corrosion and impact damage, and can be finished to reveal visual texture (kurouchi black finish, migaki polish, or Damascus-pattern cladding). San Mai construction allows the blade to combine the extreme hardness and sharpness of traditional carbon or PM steels with the structural resilience and lower maintenance of softer outer layers.
