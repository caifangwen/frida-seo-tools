Canvas Bag Labels: PU Leather, Woven & Genuine Leather Options (2026)

Why do some canvas bags look like commodities while others look like brands? Often, the difference is a 2-inch label. A carefully chosen leather patch, a precisely woven tag, or a debossed PU label transforms an anonymous tote into a product with identity, heritage, and perceived value. Labels are the smallest investment with the largest branding return in bag manufacturing.

Labels do more than display a logo. They signal quality tier (genuine leather suggests premium; woven satin suggests fashion; printed PU suggests accessible). They provide care information legally required in many markets. And they create the tactile and visual signatures that customers remember.

This guide covers every major label type for canvas bags: materials, manufacturing methods, attachment techniques, sizing standards, and placement strategies that maximize brand impact.

01

PU Leather Labels

Debossed, Embossed, Printed, and Stitched

PU (polyurethane) leather labels are the most common label type on canvas bags. They offer leather-like aesthetics at a fraction of the cost, with consistent quality and easier customization.

What is PU leather? PU leather is a synthetic material: a polyurethane coating applied to a fabric backing (usually polyester or cotton). It mimics the appearance and texture of genuine leather without using animal products.

Manufacturing techniques:

Debossed PU Labels: Debossing presses the design into the PU surface, creating a recessed impression.

  • Process: A heated metal die presses into the PU material at 120–160°C
  • Appearance: Subtle, tactile indentation; tone-on-tone or contrasting foil can be added
  • Best for: Premium positioning, minimalist branding, heritage aesthetics
  • Durability: Excellent—the impression is permanent and doesn't wear off

Embossed PU Labels: Embossing raises the design above the surface.

  • Process: Similar to debossing but the die is reversed
  • Appearance: Raised logo or pattern that catches light
  • Best for: Bold branding, textured interest
  • Note: Embossing is less common than debossing for labels; debossing generally looks more refined

Printed PU Labels: UV or screen printing applies color directly to the PU surface.

  • Process: Digital or screen printing with specialized adhesion promoters
  • Appearance: Full-color logos, detailed graphics
  • Best for: Complex multicolor designs, photo-realistic branding
  • Durability: Good, but printed surfaces can wear at high-friction contact points

Stitched Edge PU Labels: The label perimeter is stitched with contrast or matching thread for a crafted appearance.

  • Process: Sewing machine adds decorative stitching around the label edge
  • Appearance: Handmade, artisanal character
  • Best for: Heritage brands, craft positioning, bags where visible stitching reinforces quality

PU leather characteristics for labels:

Property Performance
Cost Low–Medium ($0.15–$0.60 per label)
Consistency Excellent—batch-to-batch uniformity
Water resistance Very good
UV resistance Moderate—may fade or crack after prolonged sun exposure
Flexibility Good—bends with bag without cracking
Eco-credentials Synthetic; not biodegradable; some recycled PU options emerging
Color options Wide range—can match most brand colors

Genuine Leather Labels

Full-Grain vs Split Leather

Genuine leather labels signal authenticity, heritage, and premium quality. They're the label of choice for brands where material truth matters.

Full-Grain Leather: Full-grain is the highest quality leather, using the top layer of the hide with all natural grain intact.

  • Appearance: Natural grain pattern, rich character, develops patina over time
  • Durability: Excellent—strongest and most durable leather type
  • Aging: Darkens and softens with time; scratches become part of the character
  • Best for: Premium heritage brands, bags priced $75+, authentic craft positioning

Top-Grain Leather: Top-grain is full-grain that has been sanded to remove imperfections, then finished with a protective coating.

  • Appearance: More uniform than full-grain; smoother surface
  • Durability: Very good—slightly less than full-grain but more resistant to stains
  • Best for: Consistent appearance across large production runs

Split Leather (Genuine Leather): Split leather comes from the lower layers of the hide, below the grain. It's less durable but more affordable.

  • Appearance: Suede-like or heavily finished surface; less natural character
  • Durability: Moderate—can delaminate or crack at stress points
  • Best for: Budget positioning where "genuine leather" claim matters more than longevity

Leather label manufacturing:

Technique Process Appearance Cost
Debossed Heated die presses into leather Recessed, permanent, refined Medium
Embossed Raised design Bold, dimensional Medium
Hot foil stamped Metallic foil applied with heat Shiny metallic accents Low–Medium
Laser engraved Laser burns design into surface Precise, dark contrast Medium
Screen printed Ink applied to surface Full color, less durable Low
Sewn edge Hand or machine stitching Artisanal, crafted Medium–High

Leather sourcing considerations: - Origin: Italian and Argentine leathers are premium standards; Indian and Chinese leathers vary widely in quality - Tanning: Vegetable-tanned leather is eco-friendlier but less uniform; chrome-tanned is consistent but involves heavy metals - Thickness: Label leather typically 0.8–1.5 mm; thicker leather feels more substantial but is harder to attach

03

Woven Labels

Damask, Satin, and Taffeta Weaves

Woven labels are textile labels produced on narrow looms. They're lighter than leather, more flexible, and offer different aesthetic and functional possibilities.

Damask Weave: Damask is the premium standard for woven labels. The design is woven directly into the fabric using different colored warp and weft threads.

  • Appearance: Smooth, detailed, with woven design integrated into fabric structure
  • Detail level: High—can reproduce small text and fine logos
  • Hand feel: Soft, flexible, comfortable against skin
  • Best for: Main brand labels, care labels, premium positioning
  • Cost: Medium–High ($0.10–$0.40 per label)

Satin Weave: Satin labels have a glossy, lustrous surface created by floating warp threads over multiple weft threads.

  • Appearance: Shiny, smooth, luxurious
  • Detail level: Moderate—less fine detail than damask
  • Hand feel: Very smooth, slippery
  • Best for: Fashion brands, lingerie and accessory labels, visible branding
  • Cost: Low–Medium

Taffeta Weave: Taffeta is a plain weave with a crisp, slightly stiff hand.

  • Appearance: Matte, structured, economical
  • Detail level: Basic—suitable for simple text and logos
  • Hand feel: Crisp, can be slightly scratchy
  • Best for: Care labels, size labels, content labels, budget applications
  • Cost: Low ($0.03–$0.10 per label)

Woven label formats: - Center fold: Folded in half and stitched into seam - End fold: Folded at both ends for clean edge finishing - Miter fold: Folded at corners for dimensional labels - Straight cut: Cut to size with sealed edges (ultrasonic or heat cut) - Die cut: Custom shapes cut with dies

Woven label content: - Brand name and logo - Care instructions (wash, dry, iron symbols) - Fiber content ("100% Cotton Canvas") - Country of origin - Registration numbers (for certified products)

Many markets legally require care and content labels. Woven labels are the standard delivery method for this information.

04

Attachment Methods

Sewn, Riveted, and Heat-Sealed

How a label attaches to canvas affects both aesthetics and durability.

Sewn Attachment: Stitching is the traditional and most secure attachment method.

  • Machine sewn: Industrial machines create consistent, strong stitches
  • Hand sewn: Adds artisanal character; slower and more expensive
  • Stitch types: Straight stitch (fast), zigzag (decorative), saddle stitch (premium leatherwork)
  • Thread: Polyester thread for strength; cotton for heritage aesthetic
  • Advantages: Most durable, repairable, authentic look
  • Disadvantages: Labor-intensive, visible stitch holes if removed

Riveted Attachment: Metal rivets punch through the label and canvas, creating a mechanical bond.

  • Process: Hole punched through label and bag; rivet inserted and compressed
  • Appearance: Industrial, hardware-focused, bold
  • Best for: Work bags, outdoor bags, brands with hardware-heavy aesthetic
  • Advantages: Very strong, no thread to break, distinctive look
  • Disadvantages: Creates permanent holes; difficult to remove or replace

Heat-Sealed Attachment: A heat-activated adhesive on the label backing bonds to canvas when pressed.

  • Process: Label positioned, heat press applied at 120–150°C for 5–10 seconds
  • Appearance: Clean, no visible attachment mechanism
  • Best for: Woven labels, care labels, applications where sewing would show through
  • Advantages: Fast, inexpensive, clean appearance
  • Disadvantages: Less durable than sewing; can peel with washing and flexing; not suitable for leather labels

Adhesive Backing: Pressure-sensitive adhesive (peel-and-stick) for temporary or light-duty applications.

  • Best for: Hang tags, temporary promotional labels
  • Not suitable for: Permanent bag labels; adhesive fails under stress and washing

Attachment method comparison:

Method Durability Cost Aesthetic Best For
Sewn Excellent Medium Crafted, authentic Leather labels, main branding
Riveted Excellent Medium Industrial, bold Work bags, hardware brands
Heat-sealed Good Low Clean, minimal Woven labels, care labels
Adhesive Poor Very low Temporary Hang tags, promotions

Sizing and Placement

Standard Dimensions and Positions

Label size and placement are branding decisions with practical implications.

Standard Label Sizes:

Label Type Common Dimensions Notes
Small logo patch 2 × 3 cm to 3 × 5 cm Subtle, understated
Standard logo label 3 × 5 cm to 4 × 6 cm Most common size for main branding
Large statement label 5 × 8 cm to 8 × 10 cm Bold branding, heritage positioning
Care/content label 2 × 6 cm to 3 × 8 cm Long and narrow for seam insertion

Common Placement Locations:

Exterior: - Front panel, upper corner: Most visible; standard for logo patches - Front panel, centered: Bold statement; works for large labels - Side panel: Subtle; visible when bag is carried - Top edge / flap: Visible when bag is closed; common on messenger bags - Handle attachment: Reinforces branding at a high-contact point

Interior: - Inside top hem: Standard for care labels and secondary branding - Inside pocket: Protected, discovered by engaged customers - Side seam: Hidden when bag is closed; common for content labels

Placement strategy by brand type: - Premium heritage: Exterior leather patch, upper corner; interior woven care label - Fashion/accessible: Exterior woven label or PU patch; minimal interior labeling - Work/utility: Riveted exterior label; interior care label - Eco/sustainable: Exterior recycled material patch; interior GOTS certification label

Legal requirements: Many jurisdictions require: - Fiber content labeling (FTC Textile Rules in US; EU Textile Regulation) - Country of origin - Care instructions - Flammability warnings (for certain markets)

Woven care labels are the standard compliance vehicle. Ensure your label content meets destination market requirements.

FAQ

What's the difference between debossed and embossed labels?

Debossed creates a recessed design (pressed into the surface). Embossed creates a raised design (pushed up from the surface). Debossing is more common for leather and PU labels because it looks more refined and resists wear better.

Can I use genuine leather labels on vegan bags?

Only if you want to destroy your credibility. Vegan or eco-positioned bags should use PU leather, cork, recycled material, or fabric labels. Using genuine leather on a "cruelty-free" bag is a branding contradiction.

How do I design a woven label?

Provide your supplier with: - Vector artwork (AI, EPS, PDF) - Specified colors (Pantone or thread color references) - Dimensions - Fold type (center fold, end fold, straight cut) - Content text (brand name, care symbols, fiber content)

Woven labels have resolution limitations—very fine details and small text may not reproduce clearly.

Can labels be removed and replaced?

Sewn labels can be removed with a seam ripper, though stitch holes remain. Riveted labels leave permanent holes. Heat-sealed labels can sometimes be removed with heat and adhesive solvent but may damage fabric. Plan label placement as permanent.

What's better: leather or woven labels?

Neither is objectively better—they serve different brand positions. Leather labels signal premium, durability, and heritage. Woven labels signal fashion, lightness, and textile craft. Choose based on brand identity, bag price point, and aesthetic goals.

Do labels require separate suppliers?

Often yes. Canvas bag manufacturers typically source labels from specialized label suppliers. Some full-service manufacturers offer label sourcing as part of their service. For quality control, many brands source labels separately and supply them to the bag manufacturer for attachment.

Can I get recycled or sustainable labels?

Yes. Options include: - Recycled polyester woven labels - Organic cotton labels - Cork labels (vegan leather alternative) - Recycled PU leather - Seed paper labels (plantable) - Hemp and jute labels

These options cost 20–50% more than standard labels but support sustainability positioning.

How long do PU leather labels last?

Quality PU labels last 3–5 years under normal use. Exposure to heat, UV light, and oils (from hands or lotions) accelerates aging. PU can crack or peel at edges after prolonged flexing. Genuine leather labels typically outlast PU labels, often surviving the bag's entire lifecycle.

Bottom line: Labels are the signature of a canvas bag. A well-chosen label communicates brand identity before the customer reads a word. PU leather offers the best cost-quality balance for most applications. Genuine leather commands premium positioning. Woven labels handle compliance and add textile refinement. Whatever you choose, specify clearly, attach securely, and place strategically. The right label turns a bag into a brand.
