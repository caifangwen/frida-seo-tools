DTG Digital Printing on Canvas: Process, Colors & Durability (2026)

Can you really print a photograph directly onto a canvas bag? Yes—and DTG (Direct to Garment) printing makes it possible with no screens, no setup, and no minimum order. A digital file goes straight from your computer to the bag in minutes, producing full-color images with gradients, shadows, and photographic detail that screen printing cannot economically achieve.

DTG has revolutionized the custom bag market. What once required $500 in screen setup and a 50-unit minimum can now be produced as a single piece on demand. The print-on-demand industry, which relies heavily on DTG, grew to $6.3 billion in 2024 and shows no signs of slowing.

But DTG on canvas isn't as simple as hitting "print." Canvas texture, thickness, and absorbency create challenges that T-shirt-focused DTG operators often fail to anticipate. Pre-treatment requirements are different. Color gamut is more limited than your monitor suggests. And durability—while acceptable for many applications—doesn't match screen printing.

This guide explains how DTG works on canvas, what it can and cannot do, and how to get the best results from this powerful but nuanced technology.

01

How DTG Works on Canvas

Water-Based Inkjet on Absorbent Fabric

DTG printing is essentially a specialized inkjet printer that deposits water-based textile inks directly onto fabric. The process combines precision mechanical movement with ink chemistry formulated for cellulose fibers.

The DTG printing process:

  1. Pre-treatment: Canvas receives a liquid pre-treatment solution (essential for dark fabrics)
  2. Drying: Pre-treatment is heat-set with a heat press or conveyor dryer
  3. Loading: The bag is secured to the printer's platen (printing surface)
  4. White underbase (dark canvas only): The printer lays down a white ink layer
  5. CMYK color printing: Cyan, magenta, yellow, and black inks build the image
  6. Curing: The printed bag passes through a heat tunnel or heat press to cure the ink

The printer technology: Modern DTG printers use modified Epson printheads (typically Ricoh Gen5, Epson PrecisionCore, or Brother GTX arrays) with: - Print resolutions of 600 × 600 dpi to 1200 × 1200 dpi - Variable droplet sizes (platen droplets for smooth areas, small droplets for detail) - White ink circulation systems (white pigment settles without agitation) - Automated platen height adjustment for fabric thickness

Why water-based inks: DTG inks are water-based pigment dispersions with acrylic binders. They penetrate cotton fibers and bond as water evaporates. This differs from: - Dye-based inks: Soak into fibers chemically (not used in DTG) - Solvent-based inks: Used for synthetic materials; incompatible with standard DTG systems - UV-cured inks: Harden under UV light; used for rigid substrates, not textiles

Canvas compatibility: DTG works best on 100% cotton and high-cotton blends. Canvas—being cotton—is fundamentally compatible. However, canvas texture, thickness, and weave structure create practical differences from smooth T-shirt jersey.

02

Pre-Treatment Requirements

Essential for Dark Canvas

Pre-treatment is the invisible step that makes or breaks DTG printing on dark fabrics. Without it, white ink soaks into dark canvas and disappears.

What pre-treatment does: Pre-treatment solution (typically containing calcium chloride, citric acid, and binders) is applied to the fabric surface before printing. Its functions:

  1. Creates a barrier: Prevents white ink from soaking into dark fibers
  2. Provides a base: Gives white ink something to sit on, creating opacity
  3. Improves color vibrancy: Brightens CMYK colors printed over the white base
  4. Enhances wash durability: Helps ink bond more securely to fibers

Application methods: - Manual spray: Hand-held sprayer; inconsistent coverage, common in small shops - Automatic pre-treat machine: Even, measured application; standard for professional operations - Roller coating: Used in some industrial setups; very even but requires flat fabric

Pre-treatment on canvas vs T-shirts: Canvas requires modified pre-treatment compared to jersey: - More solution needed: Canvas's texture and absorbency drink more pre-treatment - Even coverage critical: Uneven application shows as patchy white areas - Drying challenges: Thicker canvas retains moisture longer, requiring extended drying

Dark canvas pre-treatment process: 1. Apply pre-treatment evenly across print area 2. Heat press at 160–180°C for 20–40 seconds 3. Verify complete dryness (any moisture ruins white ink adhesion) 4. Print immediately, or within 24 hours (pre-treated fabric can attract dust)

Light canvas (white, natural, light colors): Pre-treatment is optional for light canvas but often used to improve color vibrancy and wash fastness. When used, lighter application is sufficient.

Common pre-treatment failures: - Too little: White ink soaks in; print looks grey and weak - Too much: Creates a stiff, crusty feel; may yellow over time - Uneven: Patchy print appearance with light and dark spots - Not fully dry: Moisture repels white ink, creating mottled effects

03

Color Gamut

RGB to CMYK Conversion Limits

The most common misunderstanding about DTG is color accuracy. What you see on screen is not what you get on fabric.

The RGB vs CMYK gap: - RGB (screen): Uses red, green, and blue light to create colors. Gamut is large; includes very bright, saturated colors. - CMYK (DTG): Uses cyan, magenta, yellow, and black ink. Gamut is smaller; cannot reproduce many bright RGB colors.

When you send an RGB image to a DTG printer, the software converts it to CMYK. Bright oranges, certain greens, deep purples, and neon shades often shift significantly.

Colors that DTG struggles with: - Bright orange and coral: CMYK lacks a true orange; must be mixed from magenta and yellow - Vivid greens: Cyan + yellow creates decent greens, but not the bright lime or emerald of RGB - Deep purples: Require heavy cyan and magenta; often print darker than expected - Neon/fluorescent: Impossible in standard CMYK; specialty neon inks exist but are rare - Metallics: No metallic capability in standard DTG

Colors that DTG handles well: - Skin tones (the strength of photographic printing) - Navy, black, grey (neutral tones) - Earth tones, pastels, muted colors - Most blues and reds - Photographic images with natural color ranges

Managing color expectations: 1. Design in CMYK: Work in CMYK color space from the start rather than converting RGB 2. Use DTG color profiles: Printer manufacturers provide ICC profiles that simulate output 3. Request test prints: Print a color chart to establish realistic expectations 4. Adjust saturation: Reduce overly saturated RGB colors by 15–30% before printing

White ink limitations: White ink on dark canvas is the weakest link in DTG color reproduction: - White ink is less opaque than plastisol white - Multiple white ink passes improve opacity but slow production - White often has a slight grey or blue tint rather than pure white - White ink consistency varies with temperature, humidity, and printhead condition

Small Batch Economy

No Setup Costs, Print-on-Demand Ready

DTG's greatest advantage is economic, not technical. It eliminates the setup costs and minimum quantities that make screen printing prohibitive for small runs.

Cost structure comparison:

Break-even point: DTG is typically cheaper than screen printing for orders under 20–50 units, depending on color count. For single-piece print-on-demand, DTG is the only practical choice.

Why DTG costs what it costs: - Ink: White ink is expensive ($150–$300 per liter). A full-coverage dark canvas print uses $1–$3 in ink. - Pre-treatment: Solution and application labor add $0.50–$1.50 per bag. - Machine time: A detailed print takes 5–15 minutes. Machine depreciation and labor accumulate. - Maintenance: Printheads require daily cleaning; white ink circulation prevents clogging.

Print-on-demand advantages: - Zero inventory: Bags are printed after orders arrive - Unlimited designs: Every bag can be different at the same unit cost - No overstock risk: No unsold inventory in obsolete designs - Personalization: Names, numbers, custom text at no extra setup cost

05

Wash Durability

30–50 Washes with Proper Curing

DTG durability has improved dramatically since early generations, but it still doesn't match screen printing. Understanding the limitations helps set appropriate expectations.

What affects DTG wash durability:

  1. Cure quality:
  2. Under-cured ink washes away or cracks
  3. Over-cured ink becomes brittle
  4. Proper cure: Ink film reaches 160–180°C for 90–180 seconds
  5. Conveyor dryers are more reliable than heat presses for consistent curing
  6. Pre-treatment quality:
  7. Proper pre-treatment significantly improves wash fastness
  8. Too little pre-treatment = poor ink adhesion
  9. Too much pre-treatment = stiff print that cracks
  10. Ink quality:
  11. Premium DTG inks (Kornit, Brother, Epson formulations) outperform budget alternatives
  12. White ink durability is typically the weak point
  13. Some inks include polymers that improve flexibility and adhesion
  14. Canvas preparation:
  15. Pre-washed canvas provides better adhesion than raw canvas
  16. Waxed or coated canvas repels water-based inks
  17. Very coarse canvas creates adhesion challenges

Expected wash life:

Condition Expected Wash Life Appearance After
Premium setup, proper cure 40–60 washes Slight fade, softening of edges
Standard setup 25–40 washes Moderate fade, some surface wear
Budget setup 15–25 washes Noticeable fade, possible cracking
Poor cure / no pre-treatment 5–15 washes Significant fade, peeling

Improving DTG durability: - Heat press finishing: After conveyor curing, a final heat press (160°C, 15 seconds) can improve adhesion - Wash instructions: Cold water, inside-out, gentle cycle, air dry - Avoid fabric softeners: They can interfere with ink bonding - Premium inks: The cost difference between budget and premium ink is small; the durability difference is significant

06

Canvas-Specific DTG Challenges

Texture Interference: Canvas weave creates peaks and valleys. DTG ink deposits on peaks but may not fully reach valleys, creating: - Slight unevenness in solid color areas - Visible fabric texture showing through prints - Reduced apparent resolution compared to smooth jersey

Mitigation: Higher print resolution (1200 dpi), slower print speed, heavier ink deposit.

Thickness and Platen Clearance: Canvas bags are thicker and more dimensional than T-shirts: - Standard platens designed for flat garments may not accommodate bag structures - Gussets, seams, and handles create height variations - Printheads can strike raised areas ("head strike"), damaging both printhead and bag

Mitigation: Specialized bag platens, careful bag positioning, reduced print speed, increased platen height.

Seam Avoidance: Printing across seams produces: - Ink pooling at seam edges - Gaps where the needle deflects - Uneven pressure causing print distortion

Best practice: Design prints to fit within flat panels, avoiding seams.

Zippers and Hardware: Metal zippers near print areas can: - Scratch printheads - Create magnetic interference with some printer sensors - Reflect heat unevenly during curing

Always position bags so hardware is clear of the print path.

Pre-treatment on Constructed Bags: Pre-treating assembled bags (rather than flat fabric) is difficult: - Automatic pre-treat machines require flat fabric feeds - Manual spraying on assembled bags is inconsistent - Some shops pre-treat fabric before sewing, then print after construction

FAQ

Can DTG print on any canvas bag?

Almost any 100% cotton or high-cotton-blend canvas bag can be DTG printed. Challenges arise with: - Waxed or coated canvas (repels water-based ink) - Very dark sulfur-dyed canvas (may react unpredictably with pre-treatment) - Bags with heavy hardware near print areas - Extremely coarse canvas (low thread count shows texture through print)

Why is DTG printing more expensive on dark canvas?

Dark canvas requires white underbase printing, which: - Doubles print time (white layer + color layer) - Consumes expensive white ink - Requires pre-treatment application and drying - Increases failure rate (white ink is harder to manage than CMYK)

Can DTG print white on white canvas?

Yes, but it's rarely necessary. White ink on white canvas is used for: - Subtle tone-on-tone designs - Special effects (glow-in-the-dark over white) - Creating texture in otherwise blank areas

How do I prepare artwork for DTG printing?
  • Resolution: 300 dpi at print size minimum
  • Color mode: CMYK (not RGB)
  • File format: PNG with transparent background, or PSD with layers
  • Avoid very fine details below 1mm—they may not reproduce on canvas texture
  • Account for canvas texture—slightly reduce contrast and saturation expectations
Does DTG work on recycled or organic canvas?

Yes, with caveats. Recycled canvas's variable fiber history can cause slight ink absorption differences. Organic canvas's natural wax content may require adjusted pre-treatment. Always test print on the specific fabric before production.

Can DTG prints be ironed?

Not directly on the print area. Ironing over DTG prints can melt or distort the ink film. If ironing is necessary, use a pressing cloth and low heat, or iron only the reverse side.

What's the largest print area possible with DTG?

Depends on the printer model. Common maximum print areas: - Desktop DTG: 30 × 40 cm (12 × 16 inches) - Industrial DTG: 40 × 60 cm (16 × 24 inches) - Some large-format systems: 60 × 90 cm (24 × 36 inches)

Bag construction often limits practical print areas more than printer capability.

Is DTG environmentally friendly?

Compared to screen printing, DTG has advantages and disadvantages: - Less water usage: No screen cleaning, minimal washup - Less waste: No unsold inventory in print-on-demand models - Less energy per piece at small volumes: No screen drying, no large dryer tunnels - Concerns: White ink contains titanium dioxide; some pre-treatments use chemicals; printhead maintenance generates waste

Overall, DTG is generally considered more eco-friendly than traditional screen printing for small runs, but less so than water-based screen printing at large volumes.

Bottom line: DTG digital printing has democratized full-color decoration for canvas bags. A single bag with a photograph-quality print is now economically viable. The technology handles complex designs that screen printing cannot touch and eliminates setup barriers for small brands and entrepreneurs. But DTG on canvas requires respect for its limitations: pre-treatment is non-negotiable on dark fabrics, color gamut doesn't match your monitor, and durability—while acceptable—falls short of screen printing. Use DTG where it shines (small runs, photographic detail, on-demand), and choose screen printing where absolute durability and economy at scale matter more.
