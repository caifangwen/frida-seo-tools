## Introduction

The most credible response to plastic pollution is not abandoning plastic—it is keeping existing plastic in circulation. **rPET (recycled polyethylene terephthalate) nonwoven bags** do exactly that, transforming post-consumer bottles into durable, reusable carriers. With brands facing increasing pressure to demonstrate circular economy commitment, rPET offers a verifiable, certified pathway to recycled content that consumers and regulators recognize.

- GRS certification requirements and what they prove
- How rPET compares to virgin PP in strength and appearance
- The 15–25% price premium and when it’s justified
- Marketing claims that resonate and comply

## rPET Material Source

### Post-Consumer Bottle Recycling Chain

The journey from discarded bottle to nonwoven bag involves multiple stages:

**1. Collection**
- PET bottles are collected through curbside recycling, deposit-return schemes, or commercial recovery programs.
- Source regions: North America, EU, and increasingly Southeast Asia.

**2. Sorting and cleaning**
- Bottles are sorted by resin type (PET vs. HDPE vs. PP) using near-infrared sensors.
- Caps and labels are removed.
- Bottles are washed to remove residues, adhesives, and contaminants.

**3. Flake production**
- Clean bottles are shredded into small flakes (5–10 mm).
- Flakes undergo float-sink separation to remove remaining cap material (PP/HDPE caps sink; PET flakes float).
- Further washing and drying prepare flakes for processing.

**4. Pelletization**
- PET flakes are melted and extruded into pellets (rPET resin).
- Pellets may undergo solid-state polymerization (SSP) to restore molecular weight lost during first use.

**5. Nonwoven conversion**
- rPET pellets are fed into spunbond or staple fiber production lines.
- The resulting nonwoven fabric is converted into bags through standard cutting, printing, and sewing processes.

**Yield**: Approximately 10–12 standard PET bottles (16.9 fl oz / 500 ml) produce enough rPET nonwoven for one standard tote bag (35×40 cm, 80 GSM).

## GRS Certification

### Global Recycled Standard Requirements

The **Global Recycled Standard (GRS)**, administered by Textile Exchange, is the most widely recognized certification for recycled content in textiles and nonwovens.

**GRS Requirements**

| Requirement | Standard | Verification Method |
|-------------|----------|---------------------|
| Recycled content | Minimum 20% (50%+ for GRS logo use) | Mass balance audit |
| Supply chain traceability | Full chain of custody | Transaction Certificates (TC) |
| Chemical restrictions | Compliant with MRSL (ZDHC) | Testing and audit |
| Social compliance | Meet local labor laws | SA8000 or BSCI audit |
| Environmental compliance | Wastewater/effluent standards | On-site audit |

**Certification process**:
1. **Application**: Company applies to accredited certification body (CU, ICEA, Control Union, etc.)
2. **Audit**: On-site inspection of facilities, records, and processes
3. **Testing**: Product testing for restricted substances
4. **Certification**: Issued for 1 year with annual renewal
5. **Transaction Certificates (TC)**: Issued for each material transfer in the supply chain

**GRS vs. RCS**: The **Recycled Claim Standard (RCS)** is a lighter alternative requiring only recycled content verification without the social and environmental requirements of GRS.

## Physical Properties vs Virgin PP

### Strength and Color Differences

rPET nonwoven behaves similarly to virgin PET nonwoven but differs from the more common PP spunbond:

| Property | Virgin PP Spunbond | rPET Nonwoven | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|-------|
| Tensile strength (MD) | 25–40 N/5cm | 30–50 N/5cm | rPET generally stronger |
| Elongation at break | 40–80% | 30–60% | rPET is slightly stiffer |
| Melting point | 160–170°C | 250–260°C | rPET more heat-resistant |
| Density | 0.90–0.91 g/cm³ | 1.38–1.40 g/cm³ | rPET heavier per m² |
| Moisture absorption | < 0.1% | 0.2–0.4% | rPET slightly more absorbent |
| Color consistency | Excellent | Good | rPET may have slight variation |

**Color considerations**:
- **White**: rPET white has a slight grayish or yellowish tint compared to virgin PP
- **Dark colors**: Black, navy, and charcoal mask recycled material color variation effectively
- **Light/pastel colors**: May require optical brighteners or blending with virgin material

**Practical impact**: For most bag applications, rPET performs equal to or better than virgin PP in strength and durability. The main trade-off is a slight weight increase and color consistency challenges in light shades.

## Price Premium

### 15–25% Material Cost Increase

Recycled content carries a cost premium reflecting collection, sorting, and processing infrastructure:

| Cost Component | Virgin PP | rPET | Premium |
|---------------|----------|------|---------|
| Raw material (per kg) | $1.10–1.40 | $1.30–1.70 | +15–25% |
| Fabric (80 GSM, per m²) | $0.45–0.65 | $0.55–0.80 | +15–25% |
| Finished bag (10K MOQ) | $0.28–0.42 | $0.34–0.50 | +15–20% |

**Why the premium?**
- Collection and sorting infrastructure costs
- Cleaning and decontamination processing
- Lower volumes compared to virgin resin (economies of scale)
- Certification and auditing costs

**When the premium is justified**:
- Brand sustainability commitments require verified recycled content
- Target market (EU, North America) expects environmental credentials
- Premium pricing of end product absorbs material cost increase
- Corporate or government procurement mandates recycled content

**When virgin PP makes sense**:
- Price-sensitive mass market
- Light color requirements where rPET tint is problematic
- Applications where certification is not valued

## Marketing Claims

### "X Bottles Recycled per Bag" Messaging

Quantified claims resonate strongly with consumers. Common approaches:

| Claim Type | Example | Requirement |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Bottle count | "Made from 10 recycled bottles" | Must be calculable and defensible |
| Percentage | "100% recycled content" | Only if entire bag is recycled material |
| Partial percentage | "50% recycled content" | Accurate if blended with virgin material |
| Process claim | "Made from recycled plastic" | Vague but defensible with documentation |

**Best practices for bottle-count claims**:
- Base calculations on standard 500 ml PET bottles
- Account for bag size, GSM, and fabric yield
- Example: 35×40 cm bag at 80 GSM = 0.112 m² × 0.080 kg/m² = 0.009 kg fabric. At 1.38 g/cm³ density and 85% yield, this equals ~0.011 kg PET = ~11 bottles.
- Round to conservative numbers: "Made from 10+ recycled bottles"

**Regulatory caution**:
- FTC Green Guides (US) require claims to be substantiated
- EU Green Claims Directive requires verification and prohibits vague terms
- Always align marketing claims with GRS/RCS certification scope

## Traceability Documentation

### TC (Transaction Certificate) Chain

Traceability is the foundation of credible recycled content claims. The TC system creates an unbroken paper trail:

**TC Chain Example**

| Stage | Company | TC Issued For | Quantity |
|-------|---------|--------------|----------|
| Bottle collection | Recycler A | rPET flakes | 10,000 kg |
| Pellet production | Processor B | rPET pellets | 9,500 kg |
| Nonwoven fabric | Mill C | rPET fabric | 8,500 kg |
| Bag conversion | Factory D | rPET bags | 50,000 pcs |
| Distribution | Brand E | Finished goods | 50,000 pcs |

**Key requirements**:
- Each seller issues TC to buyer within 30 days of shipment
- TC references certified supplier certificate number
- Quantities must balance (outputs ≤ inputs minus yield loss)
- Annual reconciliation audit verifies chain integrity

**What buyers should request**:
1. **GRS scope certificate**: Validates supplier is certified
2. **Transaction certificate**: Covers your specific order batch
3. **Test report**: Confirms physical properties and restricted substance compliance
4. **Material declaration**: States recycled content percentage by weight

**Red flags**:
- Supplier cannot provide TC within 2 weeks of request
- TC quantities do not logically match order size
- Scope certificate expired or does not cover product category
- No third-party certification; only self-declaration
