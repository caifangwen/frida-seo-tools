## Chapter 1: Understanding Biodegradability

### What Does "Biodegradable" Actually Mean?

**Biodegradation** is the breakdown of materials by microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, algae) into water, carbon dioxide (or methane), and biomass—without leaving toxic residues.

However, the term "biodegradable" is often misused:

| Term | Definition | Timeframe |
|------|------------|-----------|
| **Biodegradable** | Breaks down by microorganisms | Varies (months to decades) |
| **Compostable** | Biodegrades under specific composting conditions | 3-6 months (industrial) |
| **Oxo-degradable** | Fragments with UV/oxygen exposure | 2-5 years (creates microplastics) |
| **Hydro-degradable** | Breaks down with water exposure | Varies |

### The Biodegradation Process

```
Biodegradable Material
        ↓
Microorganism Colonization
        ↓
Enzyme Secretion
        ↓
Polymer Chain Breakdown
        ↓
Conversion to CO2 + H2O + Biomass
        ↓
No Toxic Residues
```

### Critical Conditions for Biodegradation

Biodegradation doesn't happen automatically. Specific conditions are required:

| Condition | Industrial Composting | Home Composting | Soil/Marine |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Temperature** | 55-60°C | 20-40°C | 10-25°C |
| **Humidity** | 50-60% | 40-60% | Variable |
| **Oxygen** | Aerobic | Aerobic | Variable |
| **Microorganisms** | High concentration | Moderate | Low-Moderate |
| **Time** | 90-180 days | 180-365 days | 1-5+ years |

**Key Insight**: A bag certified compostable in industrial facilities may NOT biodegrade in home compost or natural environments.

## Chapter 2: Biodegradable Materials for Non Woven Bags

### Material Options Compared

| Material | Source | Biodegradation Time | Conditions | Cost Premium |
|----------|--------|--------------------|------------|--------------|
| **PLA (Polylactic Acid)** | Corn starch, sugarcane | 6-12 months | Industrial compost | +50-80% |
| **PBAT (Polybutyrate Adipate Terephthalate)** | Petroleum-based (biodegradable polymer) | 6-12 months | Industrial compost | +60-100% |
| **PHA (Polyhydroxyalkanoates)** | Bacterial fermentation | 6-24 months | Various | +100-200% |
| **Starch Blends** | Corn, potato, cassava | 3-12 months | Industrial/Home compost | +40-70% |
| **Cellulose-based** | Wood pulp, cotton linters | 3-6 months | Various | +80-150% |
| **Jute/ Hemp Non Woven** | Natural fibers | 6-12 months | Soil/compost | +60-120% |

### PLA (Polylactic Acid)

**Pros**:
- Made from renewable resources (corn, sugarcane)
- Clear certification pathways (EN 13432, ASTM D6400)
- Good printability and aesthetics
- Established supply chain

**Cons**:
- Requires industrial composting (58-60°C)
- Won't biodegrade in home compost or marine environments
- Lower heat resistance than PP
- Competes with food crops for feedstock

**Best For**: Food packaging, retail bags where industrial composting is available

### PBAT (Polybutyrate Adipate Terephthalate)

**Pros**:
- Flexible and durable (similar to LDPE)
- Certified compostable
- Often blended with PLA for improved properties
- Good for film applications

**Cons**:
- Petroleum-based (though biodegradable)
- Higher cost than PLA
- Requires industrial composting

**Best For**: Flexible packaging, laminated non woven bags

### PHA (Polyhydroxyalkanoates)

**Pros**:
- Fully bio-based (bacterial production)
- Biodegrades in marine environments
- No food crop competition
- Excellent barrier properties

**Cons**:
- Very high cost (emerging technology)
- Limited production capacity
- Variable properties depending on formulation

**Best For**: Premium applications, marine-friendly packaging

### Starch Blends

**Pros**:
- Low cost among biodegradable options
- Home compostable (some formulations)
- Renewable feedstock
- Good for short-life applications

**Cons**:
- Lower durability than PLA/PBAT
- Moisture sensitivity
- Variable quality between suppliers

**Best For**: Promotional bags, event giveaways, short-term use

### Natural Fiber Non Wovens (Jute, Hemp, Bamboo)

**Pros**:
- Fully biodegradable in soil
- Renewable and sustainable
- Unique aesthetic appeal
- Supports agricultural economies

**Cons**:
- Higher cost
- Limited availability in non woven format
- Variable quality
- Less consistent than synthetic materials

**Best For**: Premium retail, luxury brands, specialty applications

## Chapter 3: Biodegradable Certifications Explained

### Major Certification Standards

| Certification | Region | Standard | Key Requirements |
|--------------|--------|----------|------------------|
| **EN 13432** | Europe | Packaging recoverable through composting/biodegradation | 90% degradation in 6 months at 58°C |
| **ASTM D6400** | USA | Compostable plastics | Similar to EN 13432 |
| **ISO 17088** | International | Compostable plastics | Aligns with EN 13432/ASTM D6400 |
| **OK Compost INDUSTRIAL** | Global (TÜV Austria) | Industrial composting | EN 13432 compliance |
| **OK Compost HOME** | Global (TÜV Austria) | Home composting | Degradation at ambient temperatures |
| **BPI Certified** | USA/Canada | Compostable products | ASTM D6400 compliance |
| **ABA Seedling Logo** | Australia | Compostable products | AS 4736 (industrial), AS 5810 (home) |

### Certification Testing Requirements

To achieve compostability certification, materials must pass:

#### 1. Biodegradation Test
- **Requirement**: 90% conversion to CO2 within 180 days
- **Method**: ISO 14855 or ASTM D5338
- **Conditions**: 58±2°C, controlled compost

#### 2. Disintegration Test
- **Requirement**: 90% passes through 2mm sieve after 12 weeks
- **Method**: ISO 20200 or ASTM D6954
- **Conditions**: Industrial composting simulation

#### 3. Ecotoxicity Test
- **Requirement**: No negative effects on plant growth
- **Method**: OECD 208 or equivalent
- **Assessment**: Germination rate, biomass production

#### 4. Heavy Metals & Fluorine
- **Requirement**: Below threshold limits
- **Limits**: As, Cd, Cr, Cu, Hg, Ni, Pb, Zn within specified ranges
- **Fluorine**: <100 ppm

### How to Verify Certifications

✅ **Do**:
- Check certification number on certifier's website
- Request test reports from supplier
- Verify scope covers your specific product
- Look for recognized logos (Seedling, OK Compost, BPI)

❌ **Don't**:
- Accept uncertified "biodegradable" claims
- Trust supplier-issued certificates without third-party verification
- Assume "bio-based" means biodegradable
- Confuse oxo-degradable with compostable

### Red Flags: Greenwashing to Watch For

| Claim | Reality Check |
|-------|---------------|
| "100% Biodegradable" without certification | Likely greenwashing |
| "Biodegradable in 90 days" (no conditions specified) | Misleading—depends on environment |
| "Eco-friendly biodegradable plastic" | Vague, unverifiable |
| "Oxo-biodegradable" / "Oxo-degradable" | Creates microplastics, banned in EU |
| "Made from plants" (implies biodegradable) | Bio-based ≠ biodegradable |

## Chapter 4: Degradation Timelines—The Real Data

### Laboratory vs. Real-World Degradation

Certification tests use ideal conditions. Real-world degradation varies significantly:

| Environment | PLA | PBAT | Starch Blend | PHA | Natural Fiber |
|-------------|-----|------|--------------|-----|---------------|
| **Industrial Compost (58°C)** | 60-90 days | 90-120 days | 60-90 days | 90-150 days | 60-90 days |
| **Home Compost (25-35°C)** | 12-24 months | 12-18 months | 6-12 months | 12-18 months | 6-12 months |
| **Soil Burial (20°C)** | 2-5 years | 2-4 years | 1-2 years | 1-3 years | 6-12 months |
| **Marine Water (15°C)** | 5+ years | 3-5 years | 2-3 years | 1-2 years | 6-12 months |
| **Landfill (Anaerobic)** | 10+ years | 8-10 years | 5-8 years | 5-8 years | 3-5 years |

**Critical Insight**: Without proper composting infrastructure, biodegradable bags may persist nearly as long as conventional plastics.

### Factors Affecting Degradation Rate

1. **Temperature**: Higher = faster (up to optimal range)
2. **Moisture**: 50-60% humidity ideal
3. **Microorganism Population**: Compost-rich environments degrade faster
4. **Material Thickness**: Thinner degrades faster
5. **Additives**: Some accelerate or inhibit degradation
6. **Surface Area**: Shredded/cut pieces degrade faster

### Case Study: PLA Bag Degradation Monitoring

```
Week 0-4:    No visible change, molecular weight begins decreasing
Week 4-8:    Surface erosion begins, slight discoloration
Week 8-12:   Visible fragmentation, 40-60% mass loss
Week 12-16:  Significant breakdown, 80-90% mass loss
Week 16-24:  Complete degradation to compost
```

*Conditions: Industrial composting facility, 58°C, 55% humidity, turned weekly*

## Chapter 7: Sourcing Biodegradable Non Woven Bags

### Key Supplier Qualifications

When evaluating biodegradable bag suppliers, verify:

#### 1. Certification Authenticity
- Request original certification documents
- Verify certification number with issuing body
- Ensure certification covers your specific product
- Check certification validity dates

#### 2. Material Traceability
- Raw material source documentation
- Batch tracking capability
- Supply chain transparency
- Third-party audit reports

#### 3. Quality Consistency
- ISO 9001 certification preferred
- In-house testing capability
- Quality control procedures
- Defect rate history

#### 4. Production Capacity
- Lead times for biodegradable materials
- Minimum order quantities
- Scalability for future orders
- Backup supply arrangements

### Questions to Ask Suppliers

```
□ Can you provide EN 13432 / ASTM D6400 certification for this exact product?
□ What is the biodegradable material composition (percentages)?
□ What are the recommended end-of-life disposal methods?
□ What is the shelf life, and how should bags be stored?
□ Do you offer home compostable options, or only industrial?
□ Can you provide test reports from accredited laboratories?
□ What is your defect rate and return policy?
□ Do you offer samples for testing before bulk order?
□ What is the production lead time for certified biodegradable bags?
□ Can you trace raw materials back to source?
```

### Price Negotiation Tips

1. **Volume Commitments**: Lock in better pricing with annual contracts
2. **Standard Sizes**: Use supplier's existing dies to reduce tooling costs
3. **Simplified Printing**: Fewer colors = lower cost
4. **Flexible Lead Times**: Off-peak production may reduce costs
5. **Long-Term Partnership**: Multi-year agreements can secure better pricing

## Chapter 10: Frequently Asked Questions

### Q1: Are biodegradable non woven bags better than conventional PP?

**It depends on end-of-life.** If industrial composting is available and bags are properly disposed, biodegradable bags have environmental advantages. If bags end up in landfill, conventional PP with recycling may be better. The key is matching material to infrastructure.

[→ Learn more: Are Non Woven Bags Eco Friendly](./are-non-woven-bags-eco-friendly.md)

### Q2: How long does it take for biodegradable non woven bags to decompose?

In **industrial composting**: 60-180 days depending on material.  
In **home compost**: 6-24 months.  
In **soil or marine**: 1-5+ years.  
In **landfill**: 5-10+ years (anaerobic conditions slow degradation).

### Q3: Can biodegradable non woven bags be recycled?

**Generally no.** Biodegradable plastics contaminate conventional plastic recycling streams. They should be composted, not recycled. Some facilities are developing separate bioplastic recycling, but this is not yet widespread.

### Q4: What's the difference between biodegradable and compostable?

**Compostable** is a subset of biodegradable. All compostable materials are biodegradable, but not all biodegradable materials are compostable. Compostable means the material biodegrades within specific timeframes under composting conditions, leaving no toxic residues.

### Q5: Do biodegradable bags work in home compost?

**Only if certified for home composting.** Look for OK Compost HOME, AS 5810, or similar certifications. Most "compostable" bags require industrial facilities (58°C) and won't degrade effectively in home compost (20-35°C).

### Q6: Are biodegradable non woven bags more expensive?

**Yes**, typically 50-200% more expensive than conventional PP bags:
- Starch blends: +50-70%
- PLA: +80-100%
- PBAT: +100-120%
- PHA: +150-200%

### Q7: Can biodegradable bags hold heavy items?

**Depends on the material.** PLA and PBAT have good strength (comparable to 80-90 GSM PP). Starch blends are weaker. For heavy items, consider higher GSM or reinforced handles. Always test with your specific products.

### Q8: What happens if biodegradable bags end up in landfill?

In anaerobic landfill conditions, biodegradation is extremely slow. Bags may persist for years and potentially generate methane (a potent greenhouse gas). This is why proper disposal infrastructure is critical for biodegradable bags to deliver environmental benefits.

### Q9: Are there any downsides to biodegradable non woven bags?

**Yes**:
- Higher cost
- Shorter shelf life (6-24 months vs. 5+ years for PP)
- Require specific disposal infrastructure
- May have lower durability
- Heat sensitivity (PLA softens at 50-60°C)
- Potential greenwashing in the market

### Q10: How do I verify a supplier's biodegradable claims?

1. Request certification number
2. Verify on certifier's website (TÜV Austria, BPI, etc.)
3. Ask for test reports from accredited labs
4. Check certification scope covers your product
5. Verify certification is current (not expired)
6. Look for recognized logos on products
