## Introduction

Color is one of the most visible elements of branded nonwoven bags. A precise brand color match can elevate perceived professionalism; a mismatch can undermine brand equity. Yet achieving consistent, accurate color on nonwoven fabric involves chemistry, physics, and rigorous process control. This guide walks you through everything from stock color selection to custom Pantone matching, including the standards, minimums, and pitfalls you need to know.

You’ll learn:

## Stock Colors vs Custom Dyeing

### 20+ Standard Colors Available Immediately

Most nonwoven manufacturers maintain inventory of **stock colors** that can be converted into bags with minimal lead time. Standard stock colors typically include:

| Color Category | Common Stock Options |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Neutrals | White, black, natural/beige, gray (light, dark) |
| Primary | Red, royal blue, navy, yellow, green |
| Secondary | Orange, purple, pink, lime, teal |
| Earth tones | Brown, khaki, olive, burgundy |

**Advantages of stock colors**:
- **Fast turnaround**: 7–14 days vs. 4–6 weeks for custom
- **Lower MOQ**: Often 500–1,000 bags vs. 1,000+ kg for custom
- **Proven consistency**: Colors are pre-tested and stable
- **No setup charges**: No lab dip or blending fees

**Limitations**:
- May not match your exact brand color
- Competitors may use the same stock shades
- Limited to manufacturer’s standard palette

### Custom Dyeing
Custom dyeing involves adding pigment masterbatch during the extrusion process (for spunbond) or dyeing staple fibers before web formation (for needle-punched/spunlace). This enables **any Pantone shade** but requires:

- Custom masterbatch formulation
- Lab dip approval process
- Production line setup and testing
- Higher minimum order quantities

## Pantone Matching Process

### Lab Dip Submission and Approval

Matching a Pantone reference on nonwoven PP requires a structured process:

**Step 1: Reference submission**
- Provide Pantone Coated (C) or Uncoated (U) number
- Specify target substrate (nonwoven PP has different color absorption than paper)
- Include tolerance expectations (ΔE < 1.0 for close match, ΔE < 2.0 for acceptable)

**Step 2: Lab dip preparation**
- Supplier prepares 3 small swatches (A4 size) with slight variations
- Each swatch is labeled with formulation code
- Dyeing conditions are recorded for reproducibility

**Step 3: Evaluation**
- Compare lab dips to Pantone reference under standardized lighting (D65 daylight simulator)
- Use spectrophotometer to measure ΔE (color difference)
- Evaluate under multiple light sources to check for metamerism

**Step 4: Approval or revision**
- Approve closest match (typically 1–2 rounds)
- Request revision if ΔE exceeds tolerance
- Lock approved formulation for production

**Timeline**: 5–10 business days for initial lab dips; 3–5 days per revision round.

### Color Matching Challenges on PP Nonwoven
Polypropylene presents unique color matching challenges:

- **Natural color**: Virgin PP has a slight milky/yellowish tint that affects pale colors
- **Fiber structure**: Filament surfaces reflect light differently than paper or woven fabric
- **Masterbatch dispersion**: Pigment distribution in extruded fibers must be uniform
- **Heat history**: Extrusion temperature can shift some organic pigments

**Pro tip**: For critical brand colors, request the supplier to match on the actual nonwoven substrate rather than a paper or plastic chip. Substrate-specific matching reduces surprises.

## Color Fastness Standards

### Grade 4+ for Retail Acceptance

**Color fastness** measures how well a dye resists fading or bleeding under various conditions. For retail nonwoven bags, the industry standard is **Grade 4 or higher** on the ISO 105-A02 gray scale (where 5 = no change, 1 = severe change).

| Test Type | Standard | Minimum Grade | Test Conditions |
|-----------|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Light fastness | ISO 105-B02 | Grade 4 | Xenon arc, 20+ hours |
| Rubbing fastness (dry) | ISO 105-X12 | Grade 4 | Crockmeter, 10 cycles |
| Rubbing fastness (wet) | ISO 105-X12 | Grade 3–4 | Crockmeter, wet cloth |
| Washing fastness | ISO 105-C06 | Grade 4 | 40°C, standard detergent |
| Perspiration fastness | ISO 105-E04 | Grade 4 | Acid & alkaline perspiration |

**Retail implications**: Major retailers (Walmart, Target, Tesco) require Grade 4+ for light and dry rub fastness. Failure to meet these standards can result in shipment rejection.

### Testing Best Practices
- Test on **finished bags**, not just raw fabric (sewing and handling can affect surface fibers)
- Test **after printing** if bags are printed, as inks and dyes may interact
- Document test reports with batch numbers for traceability

## MOQ Implications

### Custom Dye Minimums (Typically 1,000 kg+)

Custom color production involves fixed setup costs that must be amortized across material volume:

| Color Type | Typical MOQ | Cost Impact |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Stock color | 500–1,000 bags | Baseline pricing |
| Stock color, exact roll match | 1 roll (200–500 kg) | +5–10% |
| Custom Pantone match | 1,000–2,000 kg fabric | +15–25% |
| Custom Pantone, small batch | 500 kg (if available) | +30–50% |

**Fabric yield reference**:
- Standard tote bag (35×40 cm): ~0.15 m² fabric per bag
- 1,000 kg of 80 GSM fabric = ~12,500 m² = ~83,000 bags

This means a 1,000 kg custom color minimum translates to 50,000–80,000+ bags depending on size and construction.

### Strategies to Reduce Color MOQ
1. **Use stock colors**: Accept closest available stock shade
2. **Consolidate SKUs**: Design single-color campaigns across multiple bag styles
3. **Print the color**: Use white fabric + screen print in brand color (effective for logos and patterns)
4. **Laminate with colored film**: BOPP film can be custom-printed at lower MOQ than fabric dyeing
5. **Partner with specialized dye houses**: Some converters offer lower minimums by pooling orders

## Common Color Issues

### Metamerism
**Metamerism** occurs when two colors match under one light source but differ under another. This is common when:

- Matching Pantone on PP nonwoven (different pigment chemistry than paper inks)
- Combining fabric color with print color
- Using mixed fiber blends with different dye uptake

**Prevention**: Always evaluate lab dips under multiple light sources (D65 daylight, TL84 store lighting, incandescent home lighting). Specify metamerism tolerance in approval criteria.

### Batch Variation
Even approved formulations can vary between production runs due to:

- **Raw material differences**: PP resin lot variations affect base color
- **Temperature fluctuations**: Extrusion heat shifts pigment performance
- **Masterbatch aging**: Pigment concentration changes over storage time

**Prevention**: 
- Request production shade bands (min/max acceptable variation)
- Specify that all fabric for a single order must come from one production lot
- Maintain retained samples from each batch for future comparison

### Yellowing Over Time
White and light-colored PP nonwoven can yellow due to:

- **UV exposure**: Prolonged sunlight degrades optical brighteners
- **Heat aging**: Storage above 40°C accelerates yellowing
- **Migration**: Contact with PVC, rubber, or certain adhesives

**Prevention**: Specify UV stabilizer additive for outdoor use; store inventory in cool, dark conditions; avoid contact with PVC during packaging.
