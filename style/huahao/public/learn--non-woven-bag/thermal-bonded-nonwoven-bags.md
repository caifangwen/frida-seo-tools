Not every application demands maximum durability. ## Manufacturing Method

### Heat and Pressure Bonding of Thermoplastic Fibers

**Thermal bonding** (also called heat sealing or hot calendering) consolidates a fiber web by melting thermoplastic fibers at contact points. The process involves:

1. **Web formation**: A carded web of 100% thermoplastic fibers (typically PP, PET, or bi-component PE/PP) is laid onto a conveyor.
2. **Calendering**: The web passes between two heated rollers—one smooth, one engraved with a point or pattern.
3. **Melting & fusion**: At roller temperatures of 120–160°C, fibers at the contact points melt and fuse together.
4. **Cooling**: The bonded web is cooled by ambient air or chilled rollers, locking the fused points in place.

The bonding pattern determines fabric characteristics:

| Bond Pattern | Bond Area | Fabric Characteristics |
|-------------|-----------|------------------------|
| Diamond point | 8–15% | Soft, flexible, lower strength |
| Oval point | 12–18% | Balanced softness and strength |
| Strip/Saturn | 15–25% | Stiffer, higher strength |

### Material Requirements
Thermal bonding requires fibers with a lower melting point than the roller temperature. Common options include:

- **Homofil PP**: Single-component polypropylene (melting point ~160°C)
- **Bi-component PE/PP**: Sheath-core structure where PE sheath (melting point ~130°C) melts while PP core remains intact
- **PET with co-PET**: Modified polyester for lower-temperature bonding

## Cost Advantage

### Fastest Production Speed

Thermal bonding lines operate at **200–500 meters/minute**—the highest speeds in nonwoven production. This throughput, combined with minimal processing steps, yields the lowest fabric cost:

| Bonding Method | Line Speed | Relative Fabric Cost |
|---------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Thermal bond | 200–500 m/min | 1.0× (baseline) |
| Spunbond | 100–300 m/min | 1.2–1.5× |
| Needle-punch | 5–20 m/min | 2.0–3.0× |
| Spunlace | 20–100 m/min | 2.5–4.0× |

### Minimal Material Input
Thermal-bonded fabrics for bags typically range from **50–70 GSM**—lighter than the 80 GSM standard for spunbond shopping bags. This lower basis weight directly reduces material cost by 20–35%.

### Lower Energy Consumption
Compared to spunlace (water pumping, drying) or needle-punching (mechanical needle boards), thermal bonding uses only roller heating. Energy consumption per kilogram of fabric is 30–50% lower.

## Durability Limitations

### Lower Tear Strength
The point-bonded structure creates stress concentrators at each bond point. When force is applied, tears propagate easily between bonds. Typical performance:

- **Trapezoidal tear strength**: 8–15 N (vs. 20–35 N for equivalent-GSM spunbond)
- **Abrasion resistance**: 50–100 cycles (Martindale method) vs. 200–500 for spunbond

### Sensitive to Heat
Because the bonds are literally melted plastic, they can re-melt:

- **Safe temperature limit**: 80–100°C
- **Car interior risk**: Bags left in hot vehicles (60–70°C dashboard temperatures) may experience partial bond weakening
- **Ironing**: Never iron thermal-bonded nonwoven—the fabric will distort or stick

### Limited Reuse Cycles
While standard spunbond bags withstand 50–100 reuse cycles, thermal-bonded bags typically manage:

- **Light loads (< 3 kg)**: 15–25 cycles
- **Medium loads (3–6 kg)**: 5–10 cycles
- **Heavy loads (> 6 kg)**: 1–3 cycles (high failure risk)

## Suitable Products

### Single-Use Event Bags
Conferences, concerts, and festivals distributing informational materials benefit from thermal-bonded bags. Attendees typically carry light brochures for a single day, making durability less critical.

### Price-Sensitive Promotions
Retail chains running "spend $50, get a free bag" campaigns often choose thermal-bonded totes. The bag serves as a short-term marketing vehicle without significantly impacting margins.

### Low-Weight Retail
Pharmacies, cosmetics stores, and gift shops selling lightweight items (toiletries, accessories, cards) can use thermal-bonded bags for customer convenience without structural demands.

### Election & Awareness Campaigns
Political campaigns and NGOs distributing flyers, buttons, and pamphlets at rallies use thermal-bonded bags as disposable carriers. Unit costs below $0.15 make large-scale distribution feasible.

## When to Choose

### Choose Thermal-Bonded When:

- Budget is the primary constraint
- Contents are lightweight (< 3 kg)
- Expected use is single-event or short-term
- Branding is simple (1-color screen print)
- The bag is a giveaway rather than a product

### Avoid Thermal-Bonded When:

- Durability and reuse are brand priorities
- Contents are heavy or have sharp edges
- The bag will be exposed to heat (car storage, outdoor summer use)
- Premium brand positioning requires quality perception
- Load requirements exceed 5 kg
