## How Screen Printing Works

### Mesh, Stencil, Squeegee Process

Screen printing is a stencil-based process where ink is forced through a fine mesh onto the substrate:

**Step 1: Screen preparation**
- A polyester or nylon mesh (typically 43–120 threads/cm) is stretched over an aluminum frame.
- The mesh is coated with a light-sensitive emulsion.

**Step 2: Exposure**
- A film positive of the artwork is placed on the emulsion-coated screen.
- UV light hardens the emulsion everywhere except the image area.
- The unhardened emulsion is washed away, leaving open mesh where ink will pass through.

**Step 3: Printing**
- The screen is positioned on the nonwoven bag.
- Ink is placed at one end of the screen.
- A squeegee (rubber or polyurethane blade) pulls across the screen, pressing ink through the open mesh onto the fabric.
- The screen lifts, and the bag moves to the next station or drying rack.

**Step 4: Drying/curing**
- Printed bags pass through a conveyor dryer at 120–160°C.
- Curing time: 30–90 seconds depending on ink type and thickness.

### Registration for Multicolor
Each color requires a separate screen. For multicolor designs:

- Screens are mounted on a rotary carousel (4–12 stations)
- Each station prints one color in sequence
- Micro-registration adjustments align colors to ±0.2 mm tolerance
- Flash curing between colors prevents wet-on-wet smearing

## Ink Types for PP Nonwoven

### Solvent-Based vs Water-Based vs UV

Choosing the right ink is critical for adhesion, durability, and environmental compliance on polypropylene:

| Ink Type | Adhesion to PP | Durability | Cost | Environmental Profile |
|----------|---------------|-----------|------|----------------------|
| Solvent-based | Excellent | Excellent | Low | VOC emissions, regulated |
| Water-based | Good (with pretreatment) | Good | Low | Low VOC, eco-friendly |
| UV-curable | Very good | Very good | Medium | Instant cure, low energy |
| Plastisol | Moderate | Good | Low | PVC content, declining use |

### Solvent-Based Inks
The traditional choice for PP nonwoven:

- **Composition**: Pigment dissolved in solvent carrier (propylene glycol, alcohol blends)
- **Adhesion**: Excellent due to solvent etching of PP surface
- **Cure**: Evaporative drying at 120–140°C
- **Considerations**: VOC emissions require ventilation; flammability handling protocols

### Water-Based Inks
Growing preference for environmental reasons:

- **Composition**: Pigment dispersed in water with acrylic binder
- **Adhesion**: Requires corona or plasma pretreatment on PP for optimal bonding
- **Cure**: Water evaporation at 140–160°C (higher temp than solvent)
- **Considerations**: Longer drying time; can raise fabric grain; softer hand feel

### UV-Curable Inks
Emerging option for high-throughput lines:

- **Composition**: Pigment in photoinitiator-containing resin
- **Cure**: Instant polymerization under UV lamps (seconds)
- **Advantages**: No thermal drying, sharp detail, immediate handling
- **Considerations**: Higher ink cost; UV lamp maintenance; limited flexibility on very soft substrates

## Color Count Limitations

### 1–6 Colors Typical, Registration Challenges

Screen printing adds cost and complexity with each color:

| Color Count | Setup Complexity | Typical MOQ | Cost per Color |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 color | Simple | 500 pcs | Baseline |
| 2 colors | Moderate | 1,000 pcs | +$0.03–0.05 |
| 3 colors | Moderate | 1,000 pcs | +$0.05–0.08 |
| 4 colors | Complex | 2,000 pcs | +$0.08–0.12 |
| 5–6 colors | Very complex | 3,000 pcs | +$0.12–0.18 |
| 6+ colors | Impractical | 5,000+ pcs | +$0.18–0.25 |

### Registration Tolerances
- **Standard registration**: ±0.3 mm between colors
- **Tight registration**: ±0.15 mm (requires skilled operators, stable substrates)
- **Challenge areas**: Fine text with multiple colors, hairline borders, halftone overlays

**Design tip**: For nonwoven bags, limit designs to 1–3 colors when possible. The textured fabric surface makes tight registration difficult, and misalignment is more visible on coarse substrates than on smooth paper.

## Setup Costs & MOQ

### Screen Charges Amortization

Each screen incurs a setup cost that must be recovered across the production run:

| Cost Component | Typical Range | Notes |
|---------------|--------------|-------|
| Screen making (per color) | $30–80 | Depends on mesh and frame size |
| Film/output (per color) | $10–25 | Digital film positive |
| Setup/registration (per color) | $15–40 | Machine time and labor |
| **Total per color** | **$55–145** | One-time charge per order |

### MOQ Economics

| Order Quantity | 1-Color Unit Cost | 4-Color Unit Cost | Setup % of Total |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| 500 pcs | $0.35 | $0.65 | 25–40% |
| 1,000 pcs | $0.25 | $0.45 | 15–25% |
| 3,000 pcs | $0.18 | $0.32 | 8–12% |
| 5,000 pcs | $0.15 | $0.26 | 5–8% |
| 10,000 pcs | $0.12 | $0.22 | 3–5% |

**Key insight**: Below 1,000 pieces, setup costs dominate. For very small runs, consider heat transfer or digital printing instead.

## Durability & Wash Testing

### Rub Test and Wash Cycle Results

Screen-printed nonwoven bags undergo standardized durability testing:

**Crockmeter Rub Test (ASTM D5264)**
- Dry rub: 50 cycles at 9N pressure
- Wet rub: 20 cycles with wet cotton cloth
- **Pass criteria**: < 5% color loss (Grade 4+ on gray scale)

**Wash Durability**
- Machine wash at 40°C, standard detergent
- Results after 50 washes:

| Ink Type | Color Retention | Cracking | Recommended Use |
|----------|----------------|----------|----------------|
| Solvent-based | 90–95% | Minimal | Heavy-use bags |
| Water-based | 85–90% | Slight fading | Light-use, eco positioning |
| UV-curable | 95%+ | None | Premium, long-life bags |

**Real-world note**: Most nonwoven bags are spot-cleaned rather than machine washed. Under typical use (carrying, occasional wiping), screen prints last the full bag lifetime (50–100+ cycles).

## Design Best Practices

### Bold Lines, Limited Gradients, Adequate Trapping

Nonwoven fabric texture imposes design constraints that differ from paper or smooth plastic:

**Line Weight**
- Minimum positive line: 0.5 pt (0.18 mm)
- Minimum reverse line (knockout): 1.0 pt (0.35 mm)
- **Why**: Fine lines fill in on coarse mesh; ink spreads on fabric texture

**Gradients and Halftones**
- Avoid gradients if possible; use solid colors
- If gradients required: minimum 30 LPI halftone, 45° angle
- **Why**: Low line-screen halftones moiré on fabric weave; coarse mesh limits detail

**Trapping**
- Overlap adjacent colors by 0.3–0.5 mm
- **Why**: Compensates for registration variation and fabric movement

**Color Selection**
- Use high-contrast combinations for readability
- Avoid light ink on dark fabric (requires double hit or underbase)
- **White underbase**: For bright colors on dark bags, print white first, then color

**Artwork File Requirements**
- Vector format preferred (AI, EPS, PDF)
- Minimum resolution: 300 DPI at final print size
- Colors specified as Pantone or CMYK values
- Convert all text to outlines
