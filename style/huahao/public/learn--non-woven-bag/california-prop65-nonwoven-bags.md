## Introduction

California’s **Proposition 65**—formally the Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986—is one of the most stringent chemical disclosure laws in the United States. Unlike federal regulations that ban hazardous substances, Prop 65 requires businesses to provide **clear warnings** before exposing consumers to chemicals known to cause cancer, birth defects, or reproductive harm. For nonwoven bag exporters, this means understanding whether your products contain listed chemicals and, if so, how to comply with warning label requirements.

In this guide, you’ll learn:

- Which chemicals are listed and what safe harbor levels mean
- The specific chemicals of concern in nonwoven bags, inks, and dyes
- Short-form vs. long-form warning label requirements
- Testing protocols using CPSC-approved laboratories
- Supplier screening strategies to minimize Prop 65 risk

## Prop 65 Requirements

### Listed Chemicals and Safe Harbor Levels

The **Prop 65 chemical list** contains **900+ substances** maintained by the California Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA). Two categories exist:

- **Carcinogens**: Chemicals known to cause cancer
- **Reproductive toxicants**: Chemicals known to cause reproductive harm

**Safe Harbor Levels**
OEHHA establishes **No Significant Risk Levels (NSRLs)** for carcinogens and **Maximum Allowable Dose Levels (MADLs)** for reproductive toxicants. These represent exposure levels that do not require warnings:

- **Below safe harbor**: No warning required (but testing/documentation recommended)
- **Above safe harbor**: Warning label mandatory
- **No safe harbor established**: Any detectable exposure requires warning

**Critical distinction**: Prop 65 evaluates **exposure**, not just content. A bag containing a listed chemical may not require a warning if the exposure route (skin contact, inhalation, ingestion) is negligible.

## Relevant Chemicals

### Lead, Cadmium, Phthalates in Inks/Dyes

For nonwoven bags, the most commonly relevant Prop 65 chemicals include:

### Heavy Metals

| Chemical | Prop 65 Category | Common Source in Bags | Safe Harbor (NSRL/MADL) |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Lead | Carcinogen + Reproductive | Pigments, recycled content, PVC | 0.5 μg/day (MADL oral) |
| Cadmium | Carcinogen | Pigments (red/yellow), stabilizers | 4.1 μg/day (NSRL) |
| Chromium (hexavalent) | Carcinogen | Leather treatments, metal finishes | No NSRL established |
| Arsenic | Carcinogen | Natural fiber contaminants, pigments | No NSRL established |

**Ink and dye risks**: Lower-cost pigments, especially reds, oranges, and yellows, may contain lead or cadmium compounds. Metallic inks and foil stamping materials are additional risk points.

### Phthalates

| Chemical | Prop 65 Category | Common Source | Safe Harbor |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|-------------|
| DEHP | Reproductive | PVC plasticizers, printing inks | 410 μg/day (MADL) |
| DBP | Reproductive | Adhesives, coatings | 8.7 μg/day (MADL) |
| BBP | Reproductive | Plasticizers, vinyl components | 1,200 μg/day (MADL) |
| DINP | Reproductive | PVC plasticizers | 146 μg/day (MADL) |

**Bag component risks**: PVC windows, clear plastic overlays, certain zipper tapes, and some screen printing inks may contain phthalates.

### Other Relevant Chemicals

| Chemical | Source | Concern Level |
|----------|--------|--------------|
| Bisphenol A (BPA) | Polycarbonate components, thermal paper | Low for standard bags |
| Formaldehyde | Fabric finishes, adhesives | Moderate for treated fabrics |
| Nonylphenol | Detergent residues, wetting agents | Low for finished bags |
| Perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA) | Water/stain repellent treatments | Moderate if coated |

## Warning Label Obligations

### Short Form vs Long Form Requirements

Since 2018, OEHHA has required specific warning language and formatting:

### Long-Form Warning
Required for most products unless shelf space or label size justifies short form:

> **WARNING**: This product can expose you to chemicals including [name of one or more chemicals], which is [are] known to the State of California to cause cancer [and/or birth defects or other reproductive harm]. For more information go to www.P65Warnings.ca.gov.

**Requirements**:
- Word "WARNING" in all caps and bold
- Specific chemical name(s) identified
- Reference to www.P65Warnings.ca.gov
- Minimum type size: 6 pt (or reasonable size for product)

### Short-Form Warning
Permitted when:
- Label size is insufficient for long form
- Shelf space limits packaging size
- Product diameter < 2.5 inches

> **WARNING**: Cancer and Reproductive Harm—www.P65Warnings.ca.gov

**Requirements**:
- Word "WARNING" in all caps and bold
- Reference to www.P65Warnings.ca.gov
- No chemical name required
- Minimum type size: 6 pt; no smaller than largest consumer information font

### Warning Placement on Nonwoven Bags
For bags distributed in California:

| Distribution Method | Warning Placement |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| Individual retail sale | Printed on bag or affixed tag |
| Bulk B2B shipment | On outer packaging + packing list |
| E-commerce | Website product page + package label |
| Event giveaway | On bag or event signage |

**Safe harbor protection**: Using OEHHA-approved warning text and format provides "safe harbor" against enforcement action. Custom warning language does not.

## Testing Protocols

### CPSC-Approved Lab Testing

Prop 65 compliance testing should be conducted by laboratories with demonstrated capability:

| Test | Method | Typical Cost |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Total lead content | CPSC-CH-E1001-08 | $25–50 per sample |
| Total cadmium content | CPSC-CH-E1001-08 | $25–50 per sample |
| Phthalates (6P) | CPSC-CH-C1001-09 | $75–150 per sample |
| Extractable heavy metals | ASTM F963 / EN 71-3 | $100–200 per sample |
| Full Prop 65 screen | Custom panel (50+ chemicals) | $300–600 per sample |

**Testing strategy**:
1. **Screen new suppliers** with lead/cadmium/phthalate testing
2. **Test each color** independently (pigment formulations vary)
3. **Test components separately** when possible (fabric, ink, zipper, window)
4. **Retain samples** from each production lot for 5 years

### XRF Screening
**X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF)** analyzers provide rapid, non-destructive screening for heavy metals:

- **Advantage**: Instant results; no sample destruction; low cost per test
- **Limitation**: Surface-only analysis; cannot detect phthalates; requires confirmation for borderline results
- **Best use**: Incoming material screening at factory; spot-checking production lots

## Risk Mitigation

### Supplier Screening and Material Declarations

The most effective Prop 65 compliance strategy is prevention through supply chain control:

### Supplier Screening Checklist

| Question | Documentation Required | Red Flag |
|----------|------------------------|----------|
| Do pigments contain lead or cadmium? | Pigment composition declaration | "Cannot disclose" or "unknown" |
| Are inks phthalate-free? | Ink SDS + phthalate test report | Use of DEHP, DBP, BBP |
| Is recycled content tested for heavy metals? | Test report for recycled material | No testing on file |
| Are PVC components used? | Material specification sheet | PVC without phthalate-free certification |
| Do you supply other California distributors? | Customer reference | No California experience |

### Material Declaration Best Practices
Require suppliers to complete a **California Prop 65 Material Declaration** stating:

- All intentionally added chemicals in the product
- Whether any Prop 65 listed chemicals are present above trace levels
- Safe harbor assessment if chemicals are present
- Date and authorized signature

### Design-for-Compliance Strategies
1. **Specify lead-free and cadmium-free pigments** in purchase orders
2. **Use phthalate-free inks** (water-based or UV-curable)
3. **Avoid PVC components** unless certified phthalate-free
4. **Choose virgin PP over untested recycled content** for California-bound orders
5. **Request XRF screening** at incoming inspection

### When Warnings Are Unavoidable
If testing or supplier disclosure confirms Prop 65 chemicals above safe harbor:

- **Option A**: Apply compliant warning labels and continue distribution
- **Option B**: Reformulate to eliminate listed chemicals
- **Option C**: Restrict distribution to non-California markets

**Business impact**: Many major retailers (Amazon, Target, Walmart) require Prop 65 compliance documentation regardless of whether warnings are applied. Non-compliance can trigger delisting.
