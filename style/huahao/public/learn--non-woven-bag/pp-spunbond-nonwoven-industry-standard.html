Material Science

What Is PP Spunbond?

Polypropylene Resin Origin

PP spunbond nonwoven is produced from polypropylene (PP) resin, a thermoplastic polymer derived from propylene monomers. Polypropylene is one of the most widely produced plastics in the world, prized for its:

⚖️

Low Density

0.90–0.91 g/cm³ — lighter than water

🧪

Chemical Resistance

Resists acids, alkalis, and organic solvents

🔥

Low-Temp Processing

Melts at 160–170°C for energy efficiency

Continuous Filament Structure

Unlike staple fiber nonwovens that use short fibers (typically 38–51 mm), spunbond fabrics are made from continuous filaments. These filaments are extruded, drawn, and laid onto a conveyor belt in a random or oriented pattern, then bonded together.

Key advantage: The continuous structure gives spunbond nonwoven superior tensile strength, dimensional stability under load, and uniformity across the fabric roll.

Production

Manufacturing Process

The production of PP spunbond nonwoven involves four critical stages:

01

Extrusion

PP pellets heated to 200–230°C and forced through a spinneret with thousands of tiny holes (0.3–0.6 mm).

02

Spinning & Drawing

Filaments cooled by air and stretched to orient polymer molecules, reducing diameter to 15–30 microns.

03

Laydown

Filaments deposited on a moving conveyor in controlled patterns. Belt speed determines final GSM.

04

Bonding

Thermal calendering with engraved rollers applies heat and pressure at discrete points for strength.

Pros vs Cons

✅ Advantages

  • Lowest cost per m² among durable bag materials
  • Lightweight (60–120 GSM) for easy carrying
  • Versatile printing compatibility
  • Quick production at 100–300 m/min
  • Recyclable (#5 plastic) where facilities exist

❌ Limitations

  • Not as strong as woven PP or canvas
  • Softens above 110°C
  • UV degradation over 12+ months
  • Standard PP is not biodegradable
