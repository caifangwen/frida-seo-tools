## Introduction

A nonwoven bag is only as good as its weakest seam and only as visible as its print quality. The manufacturing process—how fabric becomes a finished bag, and how artwork becomes a permanent decoration—determines whether your bags delight customers or end up in trash bins after one use.

This guide covers the complete production workflow: three primary printing methods for decoration, two fundamental seam technologies for construction, and the handle designs that determine comfort and load capacity. We compare costs, capabilities, and limitations so you can specify the right combination for each project.

## Screen Printing: The Economical Workhorse

**Screen printing** dominates nonwoven bag decoration because it delivers opaque, vibrant colors at the lowest per-unit cost for medium-to-large runs. The process forces ink through a fine mesh stencil onto the fabric surface, with each color requiring a separate screen.

For PP nonwoven, solvent-based inks offer the best adhesion through mild surface etching. Water-based inks are increasingly popular for environmental compliance but require corona pretreatment. UV-curable inks provide instant drying and sharp detail but at higher ink cost.

The practical color limit is 4–6 colors. Beyond this, registration challenges on textured fabric and escalating screen costs make other methods more economical. Screen printing excels for bold logos, limited color palettes, and designs where durability (50–100+ wash cycles) matters.

Design constraints are real: minimum line weights of 0.5 pt, minimum text size of 8 pt, and limited gradient capability. Fine details fill in on coarse fabric, and tight registration creates visible misalignment.

## Heat Transfer Printing: Flexibility Meets Color

When designs include photographs, complex gradients, or more than four colors, **heat transfer printing** becomes the logical choice. Artwork is digitally printed in reverse onto specialty transfer paper, then bonded to the bag under heat and pressure (140–180°C, 3–6 kg/cm², 10–25 seconds).

The defining advantage is **no color limit**—a photographic image with 100 colors costs the same to transfer as a two-color logo. Small batches are economically viable because there are no screens or plates. Break-even against screen printing typically occurs at 2,000–3,000 pieces for simple designs and 5,000+ for complex artwork.

Limitations exist. The transfer film creates a perceptible stiffness on the fabric surface. Edges are visible and can catch or peel over time. Durability trails screen print: 30–50 machine washes vs. 50–100+. For event bags, gift bags, and promotional giveaways where long-term reuse is secondary to visual impact, these trade-offs are acceptable.

Direct-to-film (DTF) technology is improving hand feel and edge definition, narrowing the quality gap with screen print for medium-complexity designs.

## Laminated CMYK Printing: Magazine Quality

For brand imagery demanding photographic reproduction—product shots, intricate patterns, or complex brand photography—**laminated CMYK printing** is the only viable option. The image is gravure-printed onto BOPP film at 150–175 LPI resolution, then laminated to the nonwoven base.

This pre-lamination printing workflow achieves resolution and color accuracy impossible on fabric directly. Fine text down to 4–6 pt remains legible. Gradients are smooth without banding. Colors are vibrant because ink sits on the film surface rather than absorbing into fibers.

The cost structure is front-loaded. A four-color gravure cylinder set costs $1,200–3,200, making this method uneconomical below 3,000–5,000 pieces. But for high-volume premium campaigns, the per-unit print cost can actually fall below multicolor screen printing.

Design file requirements are stringent: 300 DPI minimum, CMYK color mode, 3 mm bleed, and vector-preferred artwork. Errors in file preparation are expensive when cylinder engraving is involved.

## Seam Construction: Welding vs Sewing

Bags must hold together under load. Two technologies dominate seam construction, with fundamentally different strengths:

### Ultrasonic Welding
High-frequency vibration (20–40 kHz) generates localized heat that melts and fuses thermoplastic materials. Cycle time is 0.5–1.5 seconds per weld. No thread, no needles, no consumables.

Welded seams are water-resistant, consistent, and clean in appearance. They excel in high-volume production where speed and low consumable cost matter. However, they fail suddenly under peel loads and cannot be repaired if the bond weakens.

### Sewn Construction
Mechanical stitching with polyester or nylon thread creates seams that fail progressively rather than catastrophically. Lock stitch (301) provides maximum security; chain stitch (401) offers speed for less critical seams.

Sewn seams are stronger for heavy-duty applications, repairable if damaged, and visually signal quality craftsmanship. They add cost through thread consumption and slower cycle times (3–10 seconds per seam vs. < 2 for welding).

**Selection guidance**: Use ultrasonic welding for high-volume, light-to-medium duty bags where speed and water resistance matter. Use sewing for heavy loads, premium positioning, or when progressive failure is preferable to sudden seam rupture.

## Handle Design: The Critical Connection

Handles are where bags live or die. Three primary constructions serve different load and budget requirements:

### Die-Cut Handles
Handles are punched directly from the bag body—no added material, no attachment labor. This is the lowest-cost option but also the weakest. A D-cut handle in 80 GSM fabric fails at approximately 6 kg. Best for lightweight giveaways and single-use events.

### Heat-Pressed Handles
Separate straps are ultrasonically or thermally welded to the bag body. Clean, fast, and water-resistant. Welded joints achieve 60–75% of fabric tensile strength. Suitable for light-to-medium loads (up to 8–10 kg) where appearance matters.

### Sewn Loop Handles
Fabric straps are stitched to the bag body with reinforcement patterns. With X-stitch or box-stitch reinforcement, sewn loops handle 15–22 kg in 80–100 GSM fabric. This is the standard for retail shopping, grocery, and any application where durability and reuse matter.

**Ergonomics matter**: Wider handles (2.5–4.0 cm) distribute pressure and improve comfort under load. Standard drop length of 32–35 cm fits most adults for hand or shoulder carry.

## Logo Placement & Print Area Strategy

Even perfect printing fails if poorly positioned. Standard practice places the primary logo on the front center panel, occupying 60–70% of available width. The back panel carries secondary information (website, tagline). Side gussets accommodate hashtags or QR codes.

Critical design constraints include:
- Maintaining 2.5–3.0 cm clearance from seams and handles
- Minimum 0.5 pt line weight and 8 pt text size for screen print
- 3 mm bleed on all edges
- Avoiding fine multicolor registration on textured fabric

Multi-sided printing increases cost by 60–80% per additional side but multiplies brand exposure. For trade shows and retail, two-sided printing is the standard.

## Production Workflow Summary

A typical nonwoven bag production workflow integrates these decisions:

1. **Material selection**: GSM and surface finish (standard vs. laminated)
2. **Cutting**: Fabric laid and die-cut into panels
3. **Printing**: Decoration applied before or after cutting depending on method
4. **Handle attachment**: Welded or sewn with specified reinforcement
5. **Body construction**: Side and bottom seams welded or sewn
6. **Finishing**: Thread trimming, inspection, folding
7. **Packaging**: Bundle packing, carton boxing, palletization

Each stage offers choices that cascade into final cost, quality, and performance. This guide provides the framework; the linked deep-dives provide the specifications.
