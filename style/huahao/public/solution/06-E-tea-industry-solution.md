## 🎯 Industry Pain Points

- **Aroma evaporation**: Tea's volatile aromatic compounds degrade within weeks of improper storage; packaging must be an aroma vault
- **Light and oxidation**: UV light and oxygen turn green teas yellow and black teas stale; barrier protection is non-negotiable
- **Moisture catastrophe**: Tea absorbs moisture from air, leading to mold, off-flavors, and complete product loss
- **Cultural authenticity**: Premium tea markets demand packaging that honors tradition — from Chinese gongfu to Japanese matcha to British afternoon
- **Gift market dominance**: 40-60% of premium tea is purchased as gifts; packaging must justify gifting price points

## ❓ FAQ

**Q1: How long will tea stay fresh in foil pouches?**
A: Properly sealed foil pouches maintain tea freshness 12-24 months for most teas. Delicate greens and matcha benefit from nitrogen flush and refrigerator storage for maximum longevity.

**Q2: Why shouldn't tea packaging have clear windows?**
A: Clear windows expose tea to light degradation, which alters color and aroma within weeks. If windows are essential for retail, we recommend nitrogen-filled compartments or UV-filtering films with minimal exposure.

**Q3: What is the best packaging for matcha powder?**
A: Nitrogen-flushed foil pouches with oxygen absorber and UV protection. Matcha is extremely sensitive to oxidation; every 1% of oxygen exposure accelerates degradation significantly.

**Q4: Can tea packaging be both traditional and sustainable?**
A: Yes. Our unbleached kraft + PLA-lined pouches with tin ties maintain traditional tea aesthetics while being compostable. Reusable ceramic canisters with refill pouches offer the most sustainable long-term solution.

**Q5: How do you preserve aroma in highly fragrant teas (jasmine, Earl Grey)?**
A: Multi-layer foil with aroma-barrier properties, nitrogen flush, and immediate sealing after filling. We also recommend smaller pouch sizes to minimize headspace oxygen exposure after opening.

**Q6: What makes tea gift packaging feel premium?**
A: Key elements: substantial rigid construction, cultural authenticity (not generic luxury), origin storytelling, sensory descriptors, and limited edition numbering. The packaging should feel like part of the tea experience, not just a container.
