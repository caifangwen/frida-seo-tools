01
Base Requirements

Base Fabric MOQ

Minimum Order Quantity (MOQ) is the smallest order a supplier will accept for a specific bag specification. Understanding why MOQs exist — and how to work within them — saves time and prevents frustration during procurement.

Typically 5,000–10,000 pcs per Size

MOQ is driven by production economics at each manufacturing stage:

Extrusion setup: Changing tape color, width, or denier requires purging extrusion lines, adjusting dies, and producing startup scrap until specifications stabilize. This setup process generates 50–200 kg of off-spec tape. For small orders, this scrap represents an unacceptable percentage of total production.

Loom setup: Threading a new pattern or density on a circular loom takes 2–4 hours of downtime. During this period, the loom produces no revenue. Suppliers need sufficient order volume to justify this setup cost.

Conversion efficiency: Printing presses, sewing stations, and cutting equipment all have optimal run lengths. Short runs mean frequent setups, more waste, and lower equipment utilization. Most conversion lines become economically efficient only above 5,000 pcs per design.

  • Plain bags (single color, standard size): 5,000–10,000 pcs MOQ
  • Flexo printed (1–2 colors): 5,000–10,000 pcs MOQ
  • BOPP laminated: 3,000–5,000 pcs MOQ (higher due to cylinder costs)
  • Custom color tape (striped): 10,000–20,000 pcs MOQ (masterbatch minimums)
  • Special sizes: 10,000+ pcs (loom rethreading required)
📦
MOQ Reduction Strategy: If your volume is below standard MOQ, ask suppliers about: (1) Combining with other customers' orders (group buying); (2) Using stock fabric in standard colors with custom printing only; (3) Accepting longer lead times to fit into production gaps; (4) Paying a small-order surcharge (10–20%) rather than buying excess inventory you don't need.
02
Tooling

Printing Cylinder Costs

Gravure cylinders represent the largest fixed cost in premium woven bag production. Understanding cylinder economics helps buyers plan budgets, evaluate design complexity, and amortize investments across multiple production runs.

$300–$800 per Color per Set

A gravure cylinder is a precision-machined steel roller engraved with microscopic cells that hold ink. Each color in a design requires a separate cylinder. Cylinder costs depend on:

  • Width: Wider cylinders cost more; standard cement/rice bag widths (35–50 cm) are at the lower end
  • Circumference: Larger circumference (repeat length) requires more engraving time
  • Line screen: Higher resolution (150+ lines/cm) requires more precise engraving and costs more
  • Engraving method: Electronic engraving is faster and cheaper than hand-engraved copper cylinders
  • Quantity: Ordering multiple cylinders simultaneously often yields volume discounts

Typical cylinder costs by market:

Cylinder Ownership: Clarify cylinder ownership in your contract. Most suppliers retain physical cylinders but grant buyers exclusive use rights for their design. Some suppliers charge storage fees after 12–24 months of inactivity. If switching suppliers, cylinder transfer is sometimes possible but requires compatibility verification. Always get written confirmation of ownership terms.

03
Finance

Amortization Strategy

The key to justifying cylinder investment is spreading the fixed cost across enough bags to make the per-unit impact acceptable. Smart buyers plan amortization before committing to cylinder engraving.

Spread Over Order Volume

Per-bag cylinder cost = Total cylinder cost ÷ Order quantity

Example: A 6-color design with $400 per cylinder = $2,400 total tooling. Spread across 10,000 bags, the cylinder adds $0.24 per bag. Spread across 50,000 bags, it adds only $0.048 per bag.

For context: a plain 50kg bag might cost $0.35 ex-works. Adding $0.24 per bag for cylinders on a 10,000-bag order increases cost by 69% — a significant premium. On a 50,000-bag order, the increase is only 14% — much more manageable.

Break-Even Rule: As a general guideline, cylinder investment becomes economically attractive when amortized over 20,000+ bags per color. Below 10,000 bags, consider flexographic printing or sticker labeling instead of BOPP gravure. Above 50,000 bags, the cylinder cost becomes negligible and gravure is clearly superior.
04
Design Economics

Color Count Impact

Each additional color in a BOPP design adds one cylinder, one setup, and additional printing complexity. The relationship between color count and total cost is not linear — it accelerates.

More Colors = Higher MOQ

Cylinder cost scales linearly: 4 colors cost twice as much as 2 colors. But MOQ often scales faster because registration difficulty increases with each added color, reducing production efficiency and increasing waste.

Typical MOQ by color count:

  • 1–2 colors: 3,000–5,000 pcs (easy registration, low waste)
  • 3–4 colors: 5,000–8,000 pcs (moderate complexity)
  • 5–6 colors: 8,000–12,000 pcs (challenging registration)
  • 7–10 colors: 10,000–20,000 pcs (expert-level production)

Design optimization: Experienced designers reduce color count without sacrificing visual impact. Techniques include: using the bag base color as a design element (saving one color), overlapping colors to create third colors (blue over yellow = green), and using halftone screens to simulate shading with a single ink.

Pro Tip: Before finalizing artwork, ask your supplier's pre-press team for a "color reduction review." They can often suggest design modifications that reduce color count by 1–2 colors while maintaining 90%+ of the original visual impact. On a 50,000-bag order, saving one color ($400 cylinder + $0.03/bag ink) can reduce total cost by $1,900+.

05
Negotiation

Negotiation Leverage

Cylinder costs and MOQs are not always fixed. Buyers with favorable characteristics can negotiate better terms than list prices suggest.

Repeat Orders, Multi-Size Programs

Repeat orders: If you commit to annual contracts with quarterly releases, suppliers may waive or discount cylinder costs on subsequent orders. Typical structure: pay full cylinder cost on first order, 50% on second order, $0 on third and subsequent orders within 12 months.

Multi-size programs: If you need the same design in multiple bag sizes (e.g., 5kg, 10kg, 25kg rice bags with identical branding), the same cylinders can often be used across sizes if the print width is compatible. This "one design, multiple SKUs" approach amortizes cylinder investment across higher total volume.

Volume commitments: Committing to 100,000+ bags annually often unlocks preferred pricing, free cylinder storage, and priority production scheduling. Suppliers value predictable volume more than one-time large orders.

Early payment: Offering 30% deposit with order (rather than standard LC terms) improves supplier cash flow and can justify MOQ reductions or cylinder discounts of 10–20%.

Leverage Factor Typical Benefit
Annual contract (repeat orders) Cylinder cost waived after 2nd order
Multi-size program Same cylinders across 2–4 sizes
100K+ annual volume 5–10% price reduction; free storage
Early payment terms 10–20% cylinder discount
Off-peak production 5–15% price reduction; lower MOQ

Negotiation Reality: Suppliers will not negotiate below their actual setup and waste costs. If a supplier agrees to an MOQ far below their stated minimum, verify they are not: (1) Substituting stock fabric rather than custom extrusion; (2) Using outdated cylinders from previous customers (risk of design contamination); or (3) Planning to subcontract to a lower-quality facility. Unrealistically low MOQs are a red flag.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I share cylinders with another buyer?

No — cylinders are engraved with your specific design and cannot be shared without design overlap. Even if two buyers want similar colors, the artwork cells are unique to each design. The only exception is generic cylinders (solid color or simple patterns) that some suppliers keep in stock for common applications. For branded designs, exclusive cylinder ownership is standard. If a supplier suggests sharing cylinders, verify that your design won't accidentally print on another customer's bags.

How long do cylinders last?

Chrome-plated gravure cylinders typically last 50,000–200,000 impressions depending on ink abrasiveness, press speed, and doctor blade pressure. For woven bag applications (relatively coarse substrate), expect 80,000–150,000 impressions. Copper cylinders (older technology) last 30,000–80,000 impressions. Well-maintained cylinders can be re-chromed once or twice, extending life significantly. Store cylinders horizontally in climate-controlled environments to prevent corrosion and distortion.

What happens to cylinders if I stop ordering?

Policies vary by supplier. Most retain cylinders for 12–24 months after your last order, then dispose of or recycle them. Some suppliers charge monthly storage fees ($5–20 per cylinder) after 12 months of inactivity. If you want cylinders returned to you, specify this in your contract and arrange freight — cylinders are heavy (10–30 kg each) and expensive to ship. For most buyers, leaving cylinders with the supplier for potential future orders is the practical choice.

Can I get BOPP lamination without gravure cylinders?

Yes — several alternatives exist: (1) Stock printed film: Some suppliers offer generic patterns or color designs pre-printed on film. You choose a stock design and only pay for lamination. (2) Flexo on film: Less common but possible for simple designs; lower quality than gravure but no cylinder cost. (3) Self-adhesive labels: Apply premium labels to plain laminated bags. (4) Digital printing: Emerging technology for very short runs (500–2,000 pcs) with no tooling but higher per-unit cost. Each alternative has quality and cost trade-offs versus gravure.
