01
Structure

Material and Construction

The fundamental difference between woven and heavy-duty plastic bags lies in how the material is formed into a load-bearing structure. Woven bags use mechanically interlaced oriented tapes; heavy-duty plastic bags use extruded blown film.

Woven Tape vs Blown Film

Woven bag construction: Polypropylene granules are extruded into flat tapes, stretched (drawn) at ratios of 6:1 to 10:1 to orient polymer chains along the tape axis, then woven on looms into a grid fabric. The weaving process creates a textile structure where load is distributed across the grid. The finished fabric is typically 60–120 GSM, with tape widths of 2–3 mm and densities of 8×8 to 14×14 threads per inch.

Heavy-duty plastic bag construction: Polyethylene (usually LDPE or LLDPE) is melted and extruded through a circular die, then inflated with air to form a thin-walled tube — the "blown film" process. The tube is collapsed, cut, and sealed to form bags. Heavy-duty variants use thicker film (100–250 microns) or multi-layer co-extrusion to increase strength. Some incorporate gussets for volume expansion.

The critical distinction: Woven fabric is a textile — mechanically assembled from oriented elements. Blown film is a monolithic membrane — a continuous sheet of polymer. This structural difference drives every performance divergence between the two formats.

  • Woven fabric: Interlaced oriented tapes; textile structure; anisotropic strength
  • Blown film: Extruded continuous membrane; monolithic structure; isotropic strength
  • Woven basis weight: 60–120 GSM (fabric only)
  • Film basis weight: 90–220 GSM (equivalent to 100–250 micron film)

Orientation Effect: The draw ratio in woven tape production aligns polymer chains along the tape axis, dramatically increasing tensile strength. A drawn PP tape achieves 350–550 MPa tensile strength; undrawn PP achieves only 30–40 MPa. Blown film is not drawn to the same degree — polymer chains remain largely random — so film strength relies on material thickness rather than molecular orientation.

02
Strength

Strength-to-Weight Ratio

Woven bags deliver dramatically higher strength per unit weight than blown film bags. This is not a marginal difference — it is typically a factor of 2 to 4, depending on specification.

Woven Advantage for Heavy Loads

Tensile strength comparison: An 80 GSM woven PP fabric achieves 1,200–1,800 N tensile strength in the warp direction. A 150-micron LDPE film (approximately 140 GSM) achieves 150–300 N tensile strength. Even at nearly double the weight, the film delivers only 10–20% of the woven fabric's strength. To match woven strength with film alone would require 400–600 micron film (380–560 GSM) — impractically thick and expensive.

Weight efficiency: A 50 kg woven cement bag uses 80–90 GSM fabric (approximately 80–90 grams of PP). A heavy-duty PE sack carrying the same load requires 200+ micron film (approximately 190+ grams of PE). The woven bag uses 50–60% less polymer material for equivalent load capacity — a significant material and cost advantage.

Load distribution: Woven fabric distributes concentrated loads across multiple tapes. A point load on blown film creates stress concentration at that point. This means woven bags handle rough handling, sharp edges, and dynamic loads better than film bags of equivalent total weight.

Basis weight 80 g/m² ~140 g/m²
Tensile strength 1,200–1,800 N 150–300 N
Strength/weight ratio 15–22.5 N/g 1.1–2.1 N/g
Typical load capacity 25–75 kg 10–25 kg
Material per 50kg bag ~85g ~200g+
⚡
The 50kg Threshold: For loads above approximately 25 kg, woven bags are essentially the only practical choice in plastic packaging. Heavy-duty PE film bags max out around 20–25 kg for reliable performance. Woven bags routinely handle 50 kg (cement, chemicals, grain) and can be engineered for 75 kg+ (sand, minerals, construction aggregates). If your product weighs more than 25 kg per unit, woven is the default choice.
03
Durability

Puncture and Tear Resistance

How bags fail matters as much as how much they can carry. Woven and film bags fail through fundamentally different mechanisms, with woven bags offering superior resistance to the most common failure modes.

Directional Strength of Woven

Tear propagation: Blown film is notoriously susceptible to tear propagation — a small nick or puncture grows rapidly under stress because there is no structure to arrest tear growth. A 1 cm cut in a PE film bag will propagate to full rupture under moderate load. In woven fabric, tears are arrested at tape boundaries; a cut may sever individual tapes but the surrounding grid structure contains damage. A woven bag with a small puncture often remains functional; a film bag with equivalent damage fails catastrophically.

Puncture resistance: Woven fabric's grid structure distributes point loads across multiple crossing tapes. A sharp object must sever multiple tapes to penetrate. Film has no such load distribution — a sharp point creates immediate localized stress exceeding film strength. Products with sharp edges (gravel, certain fertilizers, metal parts) favor woven bags by a wide margin.

Abrasion resistance: Woven fabric's textured surface provides natural abrasion resistance. The interlaced structure allows surface wear without structural compromise — worn tapes are supported by neighboring tapes. Film bags have smooth surfaces that show abrasion damage immediately, and wear thins the load-bearing membrane directly.

  • Tear containment: Woven grid arrests tears; film propagates tears freely
  • Puncture: Woven distributes point loads; film concentrates them
  • Abrasion: Woven surface wears without structural loss; film thins uniformly
  • Impact: Woven absorbs energy through tape deformation; film risks brittle fracture
04
Handling

Stackability

How bags behave on pallets, in containers, and in warehouses is a practical concern that significantly impacts logistics efficiency and safety.

Rigid Woven vs Flexible Plastic

Bag rigidity: Woven fabric has inherent rigidity from the interlaced tape structure. A filled woven bag holds its shape — sides remain relatively flat, corners stay defined. This structural integrity creates stable pallet stacks where each layer supports the next uniformly.

Blown film bags are inherently flexible and conform to contents shape. When filled, they become rounded, soft-sided containers that deform under stacking pressure. Lower bags in a pallet stack compress and bulge, creating unstable stack geometry and increasing topple risk.

Stack height: Woven bags can be stacked 6–8 layers high on pallets with reasonable stability. Film bags are typically limited to 4–5 layers before deformation compromises stack integrity. For warehouse storage and container loading, this difference translates to 20–30% more product per pallet and per container with woven bags.

Block bottom advantage: Woven bags can be manufactured with block (flat) bottoms that create self-standing, stable bases. Film bags generally have simple sealed bottoms that do not provide the same standing stability. Block bottom woven bags are the standard for retail display and automated handling systems.

Handling Property Woven Bags Heavy-Duty Film
Stack height (pallet) 6–8 layers 4–5 layers
Shape retention Excellent Poor
Block bottom option Yes (standard) Limited
Conveyor compatibility Excellent Moderate
Pallet utilization 85–92% 70–80%

Logistics Math: A 40HQ container holds approximately 25–28 pallets of woven bags (6 layers, 50 kg bags). The same container holds only 20–22 pallets of equivalent film bags (4–5 layers). At $3,000/container shipping cost, woven bags save $200–400 in freight per container — a 7–13% logistics cost reduction. Over 100 containers annually, this becomes $20,000–40,000 in savings.

05
Sustainability

Environmental Comparison

Environmental performance depends on which metrics you prioritize: material usage, recyclability, carbon footprint, or end-of-life outcomes.

Recyclability and Material Usage

Material efficiency: Woven bags use 30–50% less polymer per unit load capacity. Less material means less embodied energy, less fossil resource extraction, and less waste at end of life. On a per-kg-of-product-packaged basis, woven bags have a smaller material footprint.

Recyclability: Both PP woven bags and PE film bags are theoretically recyclable (resin codes #5 and #4 respectively). However, woven PP is more readily recycled in practice because: (1) the heavier fabric is easier to collect and sort than lightweight film; (2) PP has established recycling markets for the pelletized output; (3) film bags often contain additives and multi-layer constructions that complicate recycling. Thin PE film is frequently rejected by recycling facilities due to processing difficulties.

Carbon footprint: PP has a slightly higher carbon intensity per kg than PE (1.95 kg CO₂e/kg vs 1.80 kg CO₂e/kg). However, because woven bags use significantly less material, their per-unit carbon footprint is typically lower. A 50 kg woven bag (~85g PP) generates ~166g CO₂e. An equivalent film bag (~200g PE) generates ~360g CO₂e — more than double.

Reuse potential: Woven bags are more durable and more frequently reused for secondary applications (storage, ground cover, waste collection). Film bags are generally single-use. Reuse extends functional life and displaces the need for new packaging.

Environmental Metric Woven PP PE Film
Material per 50kg bag ~85g ~200g
Carbon footprint (per bag) ~166g CO₂e ~360g CO₂e
Recyclability (practical) Good Moderate
Reuse potential High Low
Biodegradable options Emerging Limited
🌱
Life Cycle Thinking: The most sustainable packaging is packaging that works reliably and prevents product loss. A broken bag spilling 50 kg of cement or grain wastes the entire product's production emissions — far exceeding the bag's own footprint. Woven bags' lower failure rate means fewer spills, less waste, and better overall environmental performance even before considering material efficiency.
06
Economics

Cost Trends

Raw material price volatility affects both formats, but the woven advantage in material efficiency provides a buffer against resin cost fluctuations.

Convergence as PE Prices Fluctuate

Raw material costs: PP and PE resin prices move in correlated patterns driven by oil and natural gas markets. When oil prices rise, both resins become more expensive. However, because woven bags use 50–60% less material, their cost increase per bag is proportionally smaller. A 20% resin price increase adds ~$0.05 to a woven bag but ~$0.12 to an equivalent film bag.

Production cost structure: Woven bag production involves more steps (extrusion, drawing, weaving, converting) than film bag production (extrusion, converting), giving woven bags higher fixed processing costs. However, modern circular looms operate at high speeds (80–120 rpm), and automation has reduced labor content. The gap between woven and film production costs has narrowed significantly over the past decade.

Total delivered cost: At typical 2026 prices, a plain 50 kg woven bag costs $0.25–0.35 FOB. An equivalent heavy-duty PE film bag costs $0.30–0.45 FOB — actually more expensive despite lower processing complexity, because of the material quantity required. When lamination, printing, and liners are added, woven bags' cost advantage increases further because these value-added steps are applied to less base material.

  • Plain bags: Woven $0.25–0.35; Film $0.30–0.45
  • Printed bags: Woven $0.30–0.45; Film $0.40–0.60
  • Laminated/printed: Woven $0.45–0.70; Film $0.60–0.90
  • With liner: Woven $0.35–0.50; Film $0.55–0.75

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can heavy-duty film bags ever replace woven for 50 kg loads?

Not practically. Even the heaviest PE film bags (300+ micron) struggle to match the strength, puncture resistance, and handling characteristics of 80 GSM woven fabric at equivalent cost. Some specialty applications use reinforced film (with woven scrim or fiber mesh embedded) — but these are hybrid constructions that incorporate woven elements, not pure film replacements. For loads above 25 kg, woven bags remain the industry standard across agriculture, construction, and chemicals.

Which is better for food contact applications?

Both can meet food-grade requirements when properly specified. PE film has a longer history in direct food contact (bread bags, produce bags) and is perceived as "cleaner" by some regulators. Woven PP is increasingly accepted for food contact when made with virgin resin, food-grade coatings, and compliant printing. For fine powders (flour, sugar), woven bags with PE liners are the industry standard. For liquids or wet products, PE film is preferred. The choice depends on product form, regulatory requirements in the destination market, and existing supply chain certifications.

Do woven bags work with automatic filling equipment designed for film bags?

Generally yes, with minor modifications. Valve-filling equipment designed for film valve bags can usually handle woven valve bags with adjustments to spout diameter and clamping pressure. Open-mouth filling and sewing lines work for both formats. The main difference is that woven bags are slightly stiffer, which can affect bag opening and positioning on some equipment. Most manufacturers who run both formats report minimal changeover time. If your equipment is exclusively film-optimized, request sample woven bags for compatibility testing before committing to a format switch.

Why do some buyers still choose film bags over woven?

Several reasons: (1) Lower loads: For products under 20 kg, film bags are adequate and may be cheaper; (2) Moisture barrier: PE film provides inherent moisture sealing that woven fabric lacks without lamination; (3) Transparency: Clear PE film allows visible product inspection; (4) Established supply chains: Some industries have long-used film and resist change; (5) Retail preference: For consumer-facing products, film's softer feel and flexibility may be preferred. Woven bags win on strength, durability, and heavy-load applications; film bags retain niches where flexibility, transparency, or low cost for light loads matter more.
