Manufacturing

How Stripes Are Created

Color striped woven bags achieve their patterns not through post-weave printing but through colored tapes integrated directly into the fabric during weaving. This fundamental difference creates unique advantages in durability, cost, and color permanence that printed bags cannot match.

Colored Tapes in Warp and Weft

The process begins at the extrusion stage, where PP or PE resin is mixed with color masterbatch before being extruded into flat tapes. Masterbatch is a concentrated pigment-carrier formulation (typically 20–40% pigment in PP resin) that is diluted with natural resin to achieve target colors. Standard color tapes use 1–3% masterbatch loading.

These colored tapes are then loaded onto the loom in specific sequences to create patterns. Warp-direction stripes (running lengthwise) are created by threading colored tapes in specific positions on the warp beam. Weft-direction stripes (running crosswise) are created by feeding colored tapes through the shuttle or insertion mechanism at programmed intervals.

  • Color source: Masterbatch-doped resin extruded into colored tapes
  • Warp stripes: Colored tapes positioned on loom beam in sequence
  • Weft stripes: Colored tapes inserted at intervals during weaving
  • Stripe width: Determined by number of adjacent colored tapes
  • Pattern complexity: Limited by loom programming capability
  • Colorfastness: Pigment locked in polymer matrix, highly UV stable

Color Permanence: Because pigment is dispersed throughout the tape's polymer matrix, striped colors cannot scratch off, peel, or fade through surface wear. The color goes all the way through — cut or abrade the tape, and the color remains consistent. This is fundamentally different from surface printing or coating.

02
Design

Design Possibilities

While not as flexible as printed graphics, colored tape weaving offers a surprising range of design options. Understanding these possibilities — and their limits — helps buyers create effective striped bag designs.

Width, Color Combinations, and Borders

Stripe widths: The narrowest practical stripe is one tape wide (approximately 2.5–3 mm). Wider stripes are created by grouping multiple adjacent colored tapes. Standard stripe widths range from 3 mm to 15 cm depending on loom capability and bag size. Ultra-narrow stripes (<5 mm) can appear visually "busy" on large bags and are better suited to smaller formats.

Color combinations: Most suppliers offer 10–20 standard colors including red, blue, green, yellow, orange, white, black, and natural. Custom colors require custom masterbatch production with MOQs of 200–500 kg of tape. Two-color combinations are standard; three-color bags are achievable but increase loom setup complexity.

Border and band designs: The most common striped pattern is a solid-color border (5–10 cm) at the top and bottom of the bag with a natural or single-color body. This "header/footer" design provides brand color recognition without the complexity of full-body stripes. U-shaped borders, corner accents, and vertical side stripes are also popular.

Checker and grid patterns: By combining warp and weft stripes, simple checkerboard or grid patterns emerge at tape intersections. These patterns are subtle rather than bold and work best with high-contrast color pairs (red/white, blue/white, black/yellow).

🎨
Design Tip: The most cost-effective striped design is a single-color border on a natural body. It requires minimal loom setup, uses the least colored tape, and still delivers strong brand recognition. Many global brands (fertilizer, rice, cement) use this simple two-tone approach successfully.
03
Economics

Cost Advantage

The economic case for striped bags is compelling, particularly for buyers who need color differentiation without complex graphics or who want to eliminate recurring printing costs.

No Printing Plates or Ink

Zero plate investment: Striped bags require no photopolymer plates, gravure cylinders, or screen meshes. For a new design, this eliminates $300–800 in plate costs and $50–200 in proofing expenses. For buyers who change designs frequently or test multiple markets, this savings compounds.

No ink costs: Color is created through masterbatch in the extrusion process, not through applied ink. There are no ink purchases, no color matching fees, and no ink waste during press setup. The only incremental cost is the colored masterbatch itself.

No setup waste: Printing requires startup waste while presses are adjusted and registered. Striped weaving has no equivalent waste stream — once the loom is threaded, production begins immediately with consistent color.

04
Durability

Durability Benefit

The most significant advantage of striped bags is color permanence. Where printed surfaces are vulnerable to abrasion, UV degradation, and chemical exposure, integral tape color is effectively indestructible under normal use conditions.

Color Integral to Fabric, Never Fades

Abrasion resistance: Printed ink sits on the fabric surface and can be scratched or worn away during transport, stacking, and handling. Striped tape color extends through the entire tape thickness — abrasion merely exposes more colored material. Bags that look worn after 10 handlings in printed form remain vibrant in striped form after 100+ handlings.

UV stability: Masterbatch pigments are selected for UV resistance and dispersed throughout the polymer matrix. While printed inks may fade or chalk after 6–12 months of sun exposure, striped colors retain 90%+ of their original intensity after 2+ years outdoors. This is particularly valuable for agricultural products stored at farms or construction materials on building sites.

Chemical resistance: Surface inks can react with spilled chemicals, oils, or solvents. Masterbatch pigments are encapsulated in polymer and chemically inert. A striped bag exposed to fertilizer dust, motor oil, or cleaning chemicals retains its color where printed bags may stain or discolor.

Pro Tip: For outdoor storage applications (fertilizer, construction materials, feed), striped bags often outperform printed bags in brand visibility over time. A faded printed logo becomes illegible; a striped color pattern remains recognizable even after years of UV exposure. Factor lifespan into your branding cost analysis.

05
Limitations

Limitations

Despite their advantages, striped bags have clear limitations that make them unsuitable for applications requiring detailed graphics, variable information, or complex brand identities.

Complex Graphics Impossible

No text or logos: Stripes can create color patterns but cannot form letters, numbers, or logos. The loom controls tape position, not image formation. Any text requirement — product names, weights, warnings, barcodes — requires separate printing, labeling, or tag attachment.

Limited color count: Each additional color requires additional warp beam preparation and tape inventory. Most looms handle 2–3 colors efficiently; 4+ colors become complex and error-prone. Gradient effects, shading, and photographic elements are impossible.

Fixed pattern: Once the loom is threaded for a stripe pattern, changing the design requires stopping production and rethreading — a 2–4 hour process. This makes striped bags unsuitable for applications requiring frequent design changes or variable data (lot numbers, dates, regional variants).

Color matching constraints: Masterbatch colors are blended from standard pigment sets. Matching precise Pantone or brand colors is difficult and may require custom masterbatch with high MOQs (200–500 kg). For brand-critical color matching, printing offers far greater precision.

Compliance Warning: Many products require regulatory labeling (nutritional information, hazard warnings, country of origin) that cannot be fulfilled with stripes alone. If your product requires mandatory text, striped bags must be combined with labels, tags, or supplementary printing. Verify regulatory requirements before committing to stripe-only packaging.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I combine stripes with printing?

Yes, and this is a common hybrid approach. The bag body uses colored tape stripes for brand recognition and durability, while a printed label or flexo-printed band adds text, logos, and variable information. This combines the best of both worlds: integral color durability plus printed information flexibility. The printed area can be smaller and simpler, reducing plate costs and registration challenges.

What colors are available for striped bags?

Most suppliers stock 10–20 standard colors: natural (unpigmented), white, red, blue, green, yellow, orange, black, gray, brown, and occasionally pink or purple. Custom colors require custom masterbatch with MOQs of 200–500 kg of colored tape (equivalent to 20,000–50,000 meters of tape). For small orders, choose from standard colors. For large, consistent programs, custom colors are economical.

Do colored tapes affect bag strength?

Minimal impact. Masterbatch pigments are inert fillers at low loadings (1–3%) and do not significantly alter tensile strength. Some inorganic pigments (titanium dioxide in white masterbatch) can slightly increase tape stiffness. Organic pigments have negligible mechanical effect. A striped bag at 10×10/75 GSM has essentially the same strength as an equivalent natural bag. Always verify with tensile test reports if strength is critical.

Can striped bags be laminated?

Yes, but the lamination film obscures the woven texture and can partially mask the stripe effect. Clear BOPP lamination preserves stripe visibility while adding gloss and moisture protection. Opaque or pearl lamination covers stripes entirely. If using striped bags with lamination, specify clear film and consider that the stripe effect will be less tactile (though still visible) under the smooth film surface.
