01
Technology

Flexographic Printing on Woven Fabric

Flexographic printing (flexo) is the dominant direct-printing method for woven bags, responsible for approximately 70% of all branded woven packaging globally. It uses flexible photopolymer plates mounted on rotating cylinders to transfer ink directly onto the woven fabric substrate. The process is fast, economical at volume, and well-suited to the rough, textured surface of woven polypropylene or polyethylene.

Anilox Roller and Plate System

The heart of flexo printing is the anilox roller — a precision-engineered cylinder engraved with microscopic cells that meter a controlled volume of ink onto the printing plate. Cell volume (measured in billion cubic microns per square inch, or BCM) determines ink density. Woven fabric printing typically uses anilox rollers with 200–400 lines per inch (LPI) and cell volumes of 3.5–6.0 BCM.

The printing plate is a flexible photopolymer sheet exposed through a film negative and developed to create raised image areas. Plate thickness for woven bag printing is typically 1.14–1.70 mm (45–67 mils) to achieve sufficient ink transfer without plate collapse under impression pressure. Thicker plates (2.84 mm, or 112 mils) are used for some rough-surface applications.

  • Plate material: Photopolymer (digital or analog exposure)
  • Plate thickness: 1.14–1.70 mm standard; 2.84 mm for rough surfaces
  • Anilox LPI: 200–400 lines/inch
  • Anilox cell volume: 3.5–6.0 BCM
  • Ink type: Solvent-based, water-based, or UV-cured flexo inks
  • Printing speed: 50–150 meters/minute depending on substrate and colors

Quick Fact: Flexo was originally called "aniline printing" because early inks used aniline dyes. Modern flexo inks are sophisticated formulations with pigments, resins, and additives optimized for specific substrates. Woven bag inks must adhere to low-surface-energy polyolefins while resisting abrasion, UV fading, and chemical exposure.

Constraints

Color Count Limits

The number of colors in a flexo design is the single biggest cost and complexity driver. While modern flexo presses can theoretically run 8+ colors, practical constraints on woven fabric limit most designs to 1–4 colors.

1–4 Colors Practical Maximum

Registration challenges: Each additional color requires an additional printing station, and all stations must maintain precise alignment (registration) as the fabric moves through the press at 50–150 meters per minute. Woven fabric stretches and shifts under tension, making tight registration increasingly difficult with each added color. Beyond 4 colors, registration errors become visibly unacceptable for most brand standards.

Plate costs: Each color requires a separate photopolymer plate costing $80–200 depending on size and complexity. For a 4-color design, plate costs alone reach $320–800 — a significant investment for small and medium orders.

Setup time: Each color station must be inked, registered, and waste-producing until color matching and alignment are achieved. More colors mean longer setup, more waste, and higher per-unit costs for short runs.

03
Challenges

Registration Challenges

Registration — the precise alignment of different color layers — is the Achilles' heel of woven bag flexo printing. Understanding why registration fails helps designers create artwork that tolerates minor misalignment without looking defective.

Fabric Stretch and Weave Irregularity

Substrate movement: Woven fabric is not dimensionally stable like paper or film. Under printing tension, the fabric stretches 0.5–2.0% in the machine direction and shifts laterally as weave geometry adjusts. This movement is non-uniform — some areas stretch more than others depending on local weave tightness, moisture content, and tension history.

Weave irregularity: Even high-quality woven fabric has natural variation in tape spacing (±5–10%). These variations create local distortions that cause printed lines to wobble or colors to shift relative to each other. Fine details and tight tolerances are the first casualties of this irregularity.

Typical registration tolerance: On woven fabric, experienced printers achieve ±1.0–1.5 mm registration in the machine direction and ±1.5–2.0 mm in the cross direction. This is 3–5× looser than paper printing tolerances. Designs must accommodate this variation.

04
Best Practices

Design Best Practices

Successful woven bag designs account for flexo's limitations rather than fighting them. By following established best practices, you can achieve professional results within the constraints of the process.

Bold Fonts, Solid Colors, No Fine Lines

Typography: Use bold, sans-serif fonts with minimum stroke widths of 0.5 mm (approximately 1.5 pt). Thin serif fonts, script typefaces, and hairline strokes blur or disappear on woven fabric. Recommended fonts: Helvetica Bold, Arial Black, Impact, or equivalent heavy sans-serifs. Minimum readable size for body text: 8 pt; for critical information: 10 pt.

Line weights: All lines and borders should be ≥0.5 mm thick. Fine lines (0.2–0.3 mm) break up or wobble on the woven texture. Solid blocks of color perform better than halftones or gradients, which can appear streaky or banded.

Trapping and knockouts: When two colors abut, allow 0.3–0.5 mm overlap (trap) rather than butting edges precisely. This ensures no white gaps appear if registration shifts. Avoid knockouts (one color printed inside another) smaller than 2 mm — tiny knockouts fill in or misregister.

Color selection: High-contrast combinations work best. Dark blue on white, red on yellow, black on orange — these pairings remain legible even with slight ink spread or registration drift. Avoid subtle tone-on-tone designs (light gray on white, pale blue on medium blue) that require precise ink coverage to be visible.

Design Element Recommended Avoid
Font style Bold sans-serif Thin serif, script, decorative
Minimum stroke ≥0.5 mm <0.3 mm hairlines
Body text size ≥8 pt <6 pt
Color fills Solid, high contrast Gradients, fine halftones
Trapping 0.3–0.5 mm overlap Zero-gap butting
Knockout size ≥2 mm Tiny reverse details

Pro Tip: Print your design at actual size on a desktop printer, then tape it to a sample woven bag. If details look questionable on paper, they will look worse on fabric. This simple mockup prevents 90% of flexo design mistakes before plates are ever made.

Quality Control

Proofing Process

Never approve a flexo job without physical proofing. Digital PDFs and screen mockups cannot reveal how artwork will interact with woven fabric texture, ink absorption, and registration behavior.

Strike-Offs and Approvals

Digital proof: Before plate making, your supplier should provide a digital color proof (inkjet or laser on paper) showing approximate color placement and layout. This catches major errors but does not represent final print quality.

Plate proof / strike-off: The critical approval step. The printer runs a small quantity (typically 10–50 pcs) using the actual plates, inks, and substrate. This strike-off reveals: registration behavior, ink coverage on fabric, color accuracy vs. Pantone targets, and any design elements that fail to reproduce.

Approval criteria: Evaluate strike-offs under the actual lighting conditions of your target market (retail store lighting, warehouse lighting, outdoor daylight). Check registration at multiple locations across the bag. Verify that all text is legible and all brand colors are within acceptable tolerance (typically ΔE <3 for brand-critical colors).

  • Digital proof: Layout and content verification (free or low cost)
  • Strike-off sample: Actual print on target substrate ($50–200 depending on colors)
  • Color matching: Pantone Solid Coated reference; tolerances agreed before production
  • Approval sign-off: Written approval required before bulk production begins
  • Retention sample: Approved strike-off retained as production reference

Approval Warning: Once you approve a strike-off and bulk production begins, color or registration complaints are difficult to resolve in your favor. Suppliers will reference your signed approval. Take time during proofing — rush approvals lead to expensive regrets.

Economics

Cost Drivers

Understanding flexo cost structure helps buyers make informed decisions about color count, order volume, and whether flexo is the right process for their design.

Plate Costs Per Color

Plates: $80–200 per color depending on bag width and design complexity. Digital plate exposure is faster and more consistent than analog but costs slightly more. Reusable plates can be stored for repeat orders (typical plate life: 50,000–200,000 impressions).

Inks: Solvent-based inks cost $8–15/kg; water-based inks $10–18/kg; UV inks $25–40/kg. A typical 25kg bag with 30% print coverage uses 2–5 grams of ink per bag. Ink cost per bag is typically $0.01–0.05 for simple designs.

Setup and waste: Each color change requires 50–200 meters of startup waste while the press is adjusted. For short runs, this waste represents a significant percentage of total material. Minimum efficient run length for flexo is typically 500–1,000 meters of fabric.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can flexo print photographic images on woven bags?

Technically possible using stochastic (FM) screening or coarse halftones (30–50 LPI), but results are generally poor. Woven fabric texture disrupts fine halftone dots, creating muddiness and loss of detail. For photographic images, BOPP lamination with gravure printing is the standard solution. Flexo is best reserved for logos, text, icons, and solid color fields.

How long do flexo plates last?

Photopolymer plates typically last 50,000–200,000 impressions on woven fabric, depending on plate quality, ink abrasiveness, and impression pressure. Some digital plates exceed 300,000 impressions. For repeat orders, suppliers can store your plates for 12–24 months before material degradation affects print quality. Always confirm plate storage policies and retrieval fees.

Can I match my Pantone colors exactly?

Within limitations. Flexo ink mixing can approximate most Pantone Solid Coated colors with ΔE <3 (acceptable for most brand applications). However, some colors are challenging on PP woven fabric: bright oranges, certain purples, and metallic tones. Colors also appear different on white vs. natural (translucent) fabric. Always specify substrate color and request ink draw-downs on fabric samples before plate making.

Why is my printed color different from the PDF?

Multiple factors: (1) RGB/CMYK conversion differences — provide artwork in CMYK or spot colors, not RGB; (2) Substrate color affects ink appearance — natural fabric yields different results than white; (3) Ink absorption into woven texture dulls colors compared to smooth paper; (4) Monitor calibration varies — never judge final color from screen. Always approve physical strike-offs before production.
