01
Sewn Construction

Sewn Bottom Construction

Sewn bottoms are the traditional and most widely used closure method for woven bags. They use industrial sewing machines to stitch layers of woven fabric together with heavy-duty thread, creating a mechanical bond that has served the packaging industry for decades.

Chain Stitch or Lock Stitch

Two primary stitch types dominate woven bag production:

Chain stitch (401 stitch): Formed by a single thread looped through the fabric and interlocked with itself. Chain stitch is fast to produce and requires only one spool of thread. However, if the thread breaks at any point, the entire seam can unravel like a pulled sweater thread. Chain stitch is common for economy bags and applications where seam failure mode is less critical.

Lock stitch (301 stitch): Formed by two threads (top and bottom) interlocking within the fabric layers. Lock stitch is more secure — a broken thread at one point does not cause unraveling. It provides better seam strength and is preferred for heavy-duty applications, export packaging, and products where bag failure would be costly.

  • Thread material: Polyester or polypropylene (PP thread for chemical compatibility)
  • Thread count: Typically 6–8 stitches per inch for standard bags
  • Needle size: 20–25 gauge for heavy woven fabric
  • Seam types: Plain seam, lap seam, or bound seam depending on application
  • Equipment cost: Low ($500–2,000 per sewing head)
  • Production speed: 10–25 bags/minute per operator/machine

Thread Selection: Polyester thread offers superior strength and UV resistance compared to PP thread. However, PP thread is chemically compatible with PP bags and preferred for fertilizer and chemical applications where polyester might degrade. Cotton thread is never used — it rots, weakens when wet, and lacks the tensile strength required.

02
Heat Sealing

Heat-Sealed Bottom

Heat sealing fuses woven fabric layers together using heat and pressure, creating a molecular bond without thread or needle holes. This method is increasingly popular for applications requiring moisture resistance, higher seam efficiency, and faster production.

Hot Knife Cutting and Welding

Heat sealing for woven bags typically uses hot knife or hot bar technology. A heated blade or bar (temperature: 180–260°C depending on material) contacts the folded bag bottom, melting the PP or PE tapes together under pressure. As the material cools, it solidifies into a welded seam.

For coated or laminated bags, the coating layer (PE or PP) acts as an adhesive, flowing between woven tapes to create a continuous seal. For unlaminated bags, a thin strip of PE film may be inserted at the seam to provide bonding material, or the bags may be double-folded to increase the mass of molten polymer.

  • Seal temperature: 180–260°C (PP requires higher temps than PE)
  • Dwell time: 0.5–2.0 seconds depending on material thickness
  • Pressure: 2–5 bar for consistent weld quality
  • Seal width: 10–25 mm typical
  • Equipment cost: Higher ($5,000–25,000 per machine)
  • Production speed: 20–50 bags/minute (automated lines)
⚡
Speed Advantage: Automated heat-sealing lines operate at 2–3× the speed of manual sewing stations. For high-volume orders (100,000+ pcs), this speed difference significantly reduces labor costs and lead times, often justifying the higher capital investment in sealing equipment.
03
Performance

Strength Comparison

Seam strength is measured as seam efficiency — the ratio of seam breaking strength to the base fabric's breaking strength, expressed as a percentage. Higher efficiency means the seam is closer to the fabric's inherent strength.

Seam Efficiency Testing

Standard sewn seams on woven PP achieve 60–75% seam efficiency with chain stitch and 65–80% with lock stitch. The reduction from 100% occurs because needle holes create stress concentrators and thread stretch adds compliance. Thread breaking strength and stitch density are the primary variables — heavier thread and tighter stitching improve efficiency.

Heat-sealed seams typically achieve 75–90% seam efficiency when properly executed. The welded bond distributes stress across the entire seal width rather than concentrating it at needle holes. However, heat-sealed seam quality is highly sensitive to temperature, pressure, and dwell time — improper settings can produce weak, brittle, or incomplete seals.

Economics

Speed and Cost

The choice between sewing and heat sealing involves trade-offs between capital investment, operating speed, labor requirements, and per-unit cost. The optimal choice depends on order volume, bag specification, and available equipment.

Manual Sewing vs Automatic Sealing

Labor intensity: Sewing stations require operators to feed bags, monitor stitch quality, and trim thread. Heat-sealing lines can be fully automated with conveyor feeds, reducing labor to machine monitoring and quality sampling. Labor cost savings on automated sealing can reach 50–70% for large orders.

Equipment investment: A single sewing head costs $500–2,000. An automated heat-sealing line costs $15,000–50,000. For suppliers with existing sewing infrastructure, switching to heat sealing requires significant capital commitment. Buyers should not assume all suppliers offer both methods — verify capabilities before specifying.

Energy costs: Heat sealing consumes more electricity per bag than sewing. However, the higher production speed means energy cost per bag is often comparable or lower. For off-grid or high-energy-cost regions, this factor may favor sewing.

Pro Tip: For medium-volume orders (10,000–50,000 pcs), ask suppliers about semi-automatic heat sealing. These machines use manual bag loading but automatic sealing cycles, offering 30–40% labor savings at lower capital cost than fully automated lines.

05
Selection Guide

When to Choose Each

No single sealing method is universally superior. The right choice depends on product characteristics, handling conditions, regulatory requirements, and supplier capabilities.

Heavy Duty vs Light Duty, Open Mouth vs Valve

Choose sewn when:

  • Order volume is small or irregular (sewing requires no setup for material changes)
  • Bags are unlaminated and uncoated (heat sealing works poorly on raw woven fabric)
  • Maximum seam strength is not critical (light-duty products, <20 kg)
  • Supplier has no heat-sealing capability and you cannot switch suppliers
  • Product requires easy opening by end users (sewn bags tear open more easily)

Choose heat seal when:

  • Moisture resistance is required (no needle holes = no leakage path)
  • Bags are laminated or coated (material is already compatible with heat welding)
  • Heavy loads (>25 kg) or rough handling demand maximum seam efficiency
  • High-volume orders justify automated production speed
  • Valve bags or precision-fill applications require consistent opening dimensions

Sewn: Best For

  • Small or irregular orders
  • Unlaminated bags
  • Light-duty (<20 kg)
  • Easy-opening requirements
  • Limited supplier equipment

Heat Seal: Best For

  • Moisture-sensitive products
  • Laminated/coated bags
  • Heavy duty (>25 kg)
  • High-volume production
  • Valve or precision-fill bags

Material Compatibility: Heat sealing does not work effectively on all woven bag materials. Pure PP woven fabric without coating requires very high temperatures that risk burning or embrittlement. PE-coated bags seal at lower temperatures. Always confirm material compatibility with your supplier before specifying heat sealing. Unlaminated PP bags should generally be sewn unless a PE sealing strip is added.

06
FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a bag have both sewn and heat-sealed seams?

Yes — hybrid constructions are common. For example, a bag might have a heat-sealed bottom for moisture protection and sewn side seams for structural flexibility. Or the bottom may be sewn with a heat-sealed tape overlay to cover needle holes. These hybrid approaches capture benefits from both methods but add cost and complexity. Specify clearly in your RFQ if hybrid construction is required.

How do I inspect heat-sealed seam quality?

Visual inspection checks for: uniform seal width (no narrow spots), consistent color (no brown or black burn marks), complete fusion (no unsealed gaps), and proper alignment (no wrinkles or folds trapped in seal). Peel testing (attempting to separate the seal by hand) should require significant force — easy peeling indicates under-temperature or insufficient pressure. For quantitative assessment, request seal strength test reports from your supplier.

Does heat sealing affect bag recyclability?

Heat-sealed seams are actually better for recycling than sewn seams because they contain no foreign materials (thread). A heat-sealed PP bag is 100% PP and can be recycled directly. Sewn bags contain polyester or PP thread that, while compatible, adds a small contamination factor. For maximum recyclability, heat-sealed mono-material construction is preferred.

Why do some heat-sealed bags fail at low temperatures?

Heat-sealed seams can become brittle in cold environments (<-10°C), particularly if the sealing temperature was too high during production. Overheating causes polymer degradation, creating a brittle weld that cracks under impact when cold. Properly sealed seams using optimal temperature settings maintain flexibility to -20°C or lower. If cold-climate use is expected, specify cold-temperature seam testing and request ductility verification.
