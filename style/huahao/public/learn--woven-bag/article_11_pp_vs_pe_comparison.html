Technical Specs

Physical Properties Side-by-Side

The physical properties of PP and PE woven bags determine their handling characteristics, load capacity, and suitability for different environments. While both materials are polyolefins with similar chemical resistance, their molecular structures produce measurably different performance profiles in woven tape form.

Density, Tensile Strength, and Elongation

Density directly affects yield — how many square meters of fabric you get per kilogram of resin. PP's lower density (0.90 g/cm³ vs. HDPE's 0.95 g/cm³) means approximately 5% more fabric area per kilogram. For high-volume buyers, this density advantage compounds across millions of bags into meaningful material savings.

Tensile strength in drawn tape form is comparable between the two materials, with PP holding a slight edge in absolute breaking strength (30–38 g/denier vs. 28–35 g/denier for HDPE). However, elongation at break tells a different story: PE stretches 25–50% before failure versus PP's 15–25%. This means PE bags absorb more energy during impacts and drops, while PP bags resist deformation under sustained load.

Manufacturing

Processing Differences

The extrusion and weaving processes for PP and PE woven bags follow the same fundamental steps — melting, extruding, drawing, and weaving — but operational parameters differ significantly. These differences affect production speed, energy consumption, and quality control requirements.

Extrusion Temperature and Draw Ratio

Extrusion temperature: PP requires higher extrusion temperatures (230–280°C) compared to HDPE (180–240°C). This means PE extrusion consumes less energy per kilogram and allows faster line startup. However, PP's wider processing window provides more tolerance for temperature variation, reducing defect rates in facilities with less precise temperature control.

Draw ratio: The degree to which tapes are stretched (drawn) determines final strength. PP tapes are typically drawn at ratios of 6:1 to 8:1, while HDPE tapes achieve optimal properties at 8:1 to 10:1 draw ratios. PE's higher optimal draw ratio means longer drawing ovens or slower line speeds, partially offsetting its energy advantage in extrusion.

  • Weaving speed: PP tapes run at higher circular loom speeds (120–180 rpm) because their stiffer texture resists tangling. PE tapes typically run at 100–150 rpm to prevent soft-tape jamming in heddles and reeds
  • Tape flatness: PP holds its flattened cross-section better after drawing, producing more consistent fabric. PE tapes tend to curl or roll slightly, requiring additional spreader bars or air jets during weaving
  • Shrinkage: PE has higher thermal shrinkage (3–5%) than PP (1–2%) when reheated during conversion steps like lamination or heat-sealing. This requires tighter dimensional controls in bag finishing

Production Insight: Most Asian woven bag manufacturers are optimized for PP production. Switching a PP-dedicated facility to PE requires line adjustments that add 10–15% to production costs. Specialized PE woven producers, common in Japan and Northern Europe, achieve parity through dedicated equipment and optimized workflows.

Economics

Cost Analysis

Cost is often the deciding factor in material selection, but the true cost picture extends beyond resin price per tonne. Processing efficiency, breakage rates, and application-specific performance all affect total cost of ownership.

Historical Price Trends

Over the past decade, PP and HDPE resin prices have tracked within 5–15% of each other, with PP typically commanding a slight premium. However, the spread has widened during supply disruptions: the 2021–2022 energy crisis saw PP spike to $400/tonne above HDPE due to propylene shortages, while new PE capacity in the US Gulf Coast periodically pushes HDPE below PP by $50–100/tonne.

Field Performance

Durability in Field Conditions

Laboratory properties only tell part of the story. Real-world durability depends on how materials respond to UV radiation, abrasive contents, puncture forces, and repeated handling — conditions that often diverge from controlled test environments.

UV, Abrasion, and Puncture Resistance

UV resistance: Without stabilizers, PP degrades faster than PE in sunlight because its tertiary carbon atoms are more vulnerable to free-radical attack. However, with standard HALS stabilization (0.3–0.5%), both materials achieve comparable outdoor lifespans of 12–24 months. For unprotected short-term use, PE has a modest advantage. For properly stabilized bags, the difference is negligible.

Abrasion resistance: PE's softer surface provides better abrasion resistance against rough contents like sand, gravel, and construction aggregates. PP's harder surface can develop stress points when rubbed against sharp particles. In drop-and-drag tests, PE bags show 15–20% less surface wear than equivalent-GSM PP bags.

Puncture resistance: PE's higher elongation allows it to deform around puncture points rather than tearing. In standardized puncture tests (ASTM D4833), PE woven fabric typically withstands 10–15% more force before penetration. This advantage is most pronounced with irregular, sharp-edged contents.

Decision Framework

When PP Wins, When PE Wins

Choosing between PP and PE doesn't require deep polymer chemistry knowledge — it requires matching material properties to your specific application requirements. Use this decision framework to guide selection.

Choose PP When:

  • Room-temperature handling only
  • Cost minimization is priority
  • Stable stacking is critical
  • Hot-fill or warm storage
  • Standard agriculture / construction
  • High-speed filling lines

Choose PE When:

  • Temperatures below -10°C expected
  • Frozen food cold chain
  • Rough handling / high drop risk
  • Abrasive contents (sand, gravel)
  • Flexible drape is needed
  • Quiet handling preferred

Common Mistake: Don't default to PP for all applications just because it's cheaper. In cold climates, the breakage, replacement, and customer complaint costs of using PP where PE is appropriate typically exceed any material savings by 3–5×. Match the material to the environment, not just the budget.

Advanced Options

Hybrid Options

For applications where neither pure PP nor pure PE provides the ideal balance, hybrid constructions offer tailored solutions. These advanced options combine materials at the tape, fabric, or bag level to capture benefits from both polymers.

Co-Extruded and Coated Blends

Co-extruded tapes: Multi-layer extrusion produces tapes with a PP core (for strength and stiffness) and PE skin layers (for flexibility and abrasion resistance). These tapes require specialized extrusion dies and are produced by a limited number of manufacturers, but they deliver a true middle-ground material.

PE-coated PP fabric: The most common hybrid approach applies a thin PE coating (10–20 microns) to standard PP woven fabric. This preserves PP's structural benefits while adding PE's moisture barrier and surface softness. Commonly used for fertilizer bags and products requiring moderate chemical protection.

PP/PE blended resin: Some producers blend PP and PE resins (typically 80/20 or 70/30 ratios) to create single-material tapes with intermediate properties. However, because PP and PE are immiscible, these blends require compatibilizer additives and careful processing control. Quality consistency can vary between batches.

  • Co-extruded tapes: Best balance of properties but +20–30% cost premium and limited supplier availability
  • PE-coated PP: Cost-effective hybrid (+5–10% cost) with good moisture protection; widely available
  • Blended resin: Intermediate properties at moderate cost; batch consistency requires strong QC
⚗️
Recycling Note: Hybrid bags are more difficult to recycle than pure-material bags. PE-coated PP must be separated before recycling, and co-extruded tapes often end up in downcycling streams. If sustainability and recyclability are priorities, specify mono-material construction.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch from PP to PE without changing bag dimensions?

Generally yes, but with adjustments. PE's higher density means a 25kg load in an 80 GSM PE bag will have slightly smaller dimensions than in a PP bag if GSM is held constant. If exact dimensions are critical, increase PE GSM by 5–8% to match PP bag volume. Also account for PE's greater thermal shrinkage during heat-sealing — finished bag dimensions may be 1–2% smaller than PP equivalents.

Which material is more environmentally friendly?

Neither has a decisive environmental advantage. Both are 100% recyclable (#5 for PP, #2 for HDPE). PP's lower density means less material per bag, reducing carbon footprint for equivalent performance. PE's longer lifespan in cold applications may reduce replacement rates. The most sustainable choice is the material that performs best in your specific application, minimizing waste from breakage and premature failure.

Do PE bags require different printing inks?

Yes. PE's lower surface energy and softer texture require inks with better adhesion and lower cure temperatures. Standard PP flexographic inks may flake or crack on PE. Rotogravure and BOPP lamination processes are compatible with both materials. If switching from PP to PE with direct printing, work with your printer to reformulate inks and verify adhesion through tape tests (ASTM D3359).

Is one material better for waterproof lamination?

Both accept BOPP and PE lamination equally well. PP's higher melting point provides a wider lamination temperature window, making it slightly more forgiving in production. PE requires more precise temperature control to prevent substrate distortion. In the finished product, both provide equivalent moisture barrier performance when properly laminated (water vapor transmission rate <5 g/m²/day).
