Bale Packing

Bale Packing

Bale packing is the most common export configuration for woven bags. Bags are compressed into dense rectangular blocks and secured with plastic strapping or wire, creating handling units that are efficient to load, unload, and inventory.

500–1,000 pcs Compressed Bales

Standard bale dimensions vary by bag size and compression ratio, but typical configurations are:

  • Small bags (5–10 kg): 1,000 pcs per bale; dimensions approximately 80×60×80 cm; weight 80–120 kg
  • Medium bags (25 kg): 500 pcs per bale; dimensions approximately 90×70×90 cm; weight 150–200 kg
  • Large bags (50 kg): 250–500 pcs per bale; dimensions approximately 100×80×100 cm; weight 200–300 kg

Compression reduces bag volume by 40–60%, dramatically improving container cube utilization. Uncompressed bags would fill a container with significant air gaps; baled packing achieves 85–95% cube efficiency.

Bale construction: Bags are stacked in alternating layers, compressed mechanically or hydraulically, and wrapped with 5–8 bands of PP strapping (15–19 mm width, 0.8–1.0 mm thickness). Some exporters add corner protectors to prevent strap cutting into bale edges. For moisture-sensitive products, bales may be wrapped in stretch film or placed inside polypropylene outer covers.

Bale Weight Limit: Keep individual bale weights below 300 kg for manual handling compatibility. Heavier bales require forklift handling at both origin and destination, limiting flexibility for buyers without equipment. For developing market destinations, 150–200 kg bales are preferred.

02
Palletization

Palletization

For buyers with forklift infrastructure, palletized shipments offer faster unloading, better inventory management, and reduced handling damage. Palletization is standard for European, North American, and industrial Asian destinations.

Standard Pallet Dimensions and Heights

Pallet types:

  • Euro pallet (EUR): 1,200×800 mm; standard for European markets
  • ISO pallet: 1,200×1,000 mm; common for Asian and Middle Eastern markets
  • US pallet: 48×40 inches (1,219×1,016 mm); standard for North America
  • Plastic pallets: Reusable, ISPM-15 exempt, preferred for food and pharmaceutical

Stack height: Maximum safe pallet height for woven bag bales is 1.8–2.2 meters (including pallet). Higher stacks risk toppling during forklift transport and may exceed warehouse racking limits. Container door height (2.26–2.59 m depending on container type) sets the absolute maximum.

Weight limits: Standard wooden pallets support 1,000–1,500 kg. Heavy-duty plastic pallets support 2,000+ kg. Do not exceed pallet rated capacity — overloaded pallets break during handling, causing cascade failures.

ISPM-15 Compliance: Wooden pallets and packaging must be heat-treated (HT) or fumigated (MB) and marked with the ISPM-15 compliance stamp for international shipment. Without proper marking, wooden pallets may be rejected at destination ports or require expensive emergency treatment. Plastic pallets are exempt from ISPM-15 and increasingly preferred for export.

Container Optimization

Container Optimization

Container loading efficiency directly affects freight cost per bag. Poor loading leaves empty space that buyers pay to transport. Proper planning maximizes both weight and cube utilization.

20GP vs 40HQ Loading Plans

20-foot General Purpose (20' GP):

  • Internal dimensions: 5.9×2.35×2.39 m
  • Cube capacity: 33 m³
  • Weight capacity: 21–28 MT (varies by shipping line)
  • Typical woven bag load: 10–16 MT (weight-limited, not cube-limited)
  • Door opening: 2.34×2.28 m

40-foot High Cube (40' HC):

  • Internal dimensions: 12.0×2.35×2.69 m
  • Cube capacity: 76 m³
  • Weight capacity: 26–30 MT
  • Typical woven bag load: 22–28 MT
  • Door opening: 2.34×2.58 m

Loading strategy: Woven bags are typically weight-limited rather than cube-limited because compressed bales are dense. A 40' HC filled to 28 MT of woven bags uses only 50–60% of available cube — the remaining space is air. This makes weight optimization more important than cube optimization for woven bag shipments.

Cost Optimization: Because woven bags weight-out before cubing-out, the cost per bag is lower in 40' HC containers than 20' GP containers (assuming you have enough volume to fill them). Freight cost per TEU is not linear — a 40' HC typically costs 1.6–1.8× a 20' GP but carries 2× the weight. Consolidate orders to use 40' HC whenever possible.

Transit Protection

Moisture Protection During Transit

Ocean freight exposes cargo to humidity, condensation, and temperature swings. Woven bags — even laminated ones — are vulnerable to container sweat and seawater ingress if not properly protected.

Desiccants, Ventilation

Container sweat (rain): Temperature differences between day and night cause moisture to condense on container ceilings and walls, dripping onto cargo. This is particularly severe on routes with large temperature differentials (Asia→Europe, transpacific).

Desiccant placement: Calcium chloride desiccant bags absorb airborne moisture. Standard placement: 2 kg desiccant per 20' container, 4 kg per 40' container. Position desiccants near the container ceiling where condensation forms. For hygroscopic products (sugar, fertilizer, cement), double the desiccant quantity.

Ventilation: Some containers have ventilation openings for breathable cargo. However, ventilation also admits humid air — for woven bags with moisture-sensitive contents, sealed containers with desiccants are preferred over ventilated containers.

Container condition: Inspect containers before loading for: holes in walls/roof, damaged door seals, and floor contamination. Reject damaged containers — moisture ingress through small holes can destroy entire shipments.

  • Desiccant quantity: 2 kg/20'; 4 kg/40' (standard); double for hygroscopic cargo
  • Container inspection: Check for holes, seal integrity, floor condition
  • Loading conditions: Load in dry weather; avoid rain exposure during stuffing
  • Cargo cover: Plastic sheeting over top layers for additional protection

Documentation

Documentation

Proper documentation ensures smooth customs clearance, accurate freight billing, and traceability. Missing or incorrect documents cause delays, storage fees, and potential cargo detention.

Packing List, Weight Declaration

Commercial Invoice: Product description, quantity, unit price, total value, Incoterms, payment terms. Value must be accurate — customs authorities cross-check against market prices for duty assessment.

Packing List: Detailed breakdown of cargo by bale or pallet: number of bales, pieces per bale, gross weight, net weight, and dimensions per bale. This document is essential for receivers to verify complete delivery.

Bill of Lading (B/L): Contract of carriage between shipper and carrier. Can be original (negotiable) or telex release (electronic). Ensure B/L details match commercial invoice and packing list exactly.

Certificate of Origin (CO): Required for preferential tariff treatment under free trade agreements (FTA). Form A (GSP), Form E (ASEAN-China), and USMCA certificates are common examples.

Additional documents (as required): Phytosanitary certificate (for agricultural products), fumigation certificate (for wooden packaging), quality inspection certificate, and MSDS (for chemical products).

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I ship palletized or floor-loaded?

Floor-loading maximizes weight capacity (no pallet weight consuming container allowance) and is standard for woven bag bales. A 40' HC can carry 28+ MT floor-loaded versus 26–27 MT palletized. However, palletized unloading is faster and requires less labor at destination. Choose floor-loading when: the buyer has no forklift, labor costs are low at destination, and maximizing shipment weight is critical. Choose palletization when: the buyer has forklift infrastructure, fast unloading is important, and the destination charges high demurrage for slow unloading.

How do I prevent cargo shifting during transit?

Cargo shift causes bag damage and container imbalance. Prevention measures: (1) Pack bales tightly with minimal gaps — use dunnage (airbags, foam, cardboard) to fill voids; (2) Secure top layers with cargo nets or lashing straps anchored to container lashing points; (3) Load heaviest bales at the bottom and toward the front (near container doors for balance); (4) For partial container loads, use load bars or inflatable dunnage bags to prevent forward/backward movement; (5) Never leave loose bags unsecured — they will shift and tear.

What Incoterms are best for woven bag exports?

Most woven bag exports use FOB (Free On Board) or CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight). FOB places responsibility on the buyer once goods cross the ship's rail; CIF includes freight and insurance to destination port. For new buyer relationships, CIF offers simplicity — the supplier handles shipping logistics. For experienced buyers with freight forwarders, FOB gives more control and often lower total cost. Avoid EXW (Ex Works) unless the buyer has strong logistics presence at origin — it creates too much complexity for most woven bag transactions. DAP (Delivered at Place) is growing for door-to-door convenience.

How do I handle customs inspections?

Customs inspections are random or triggered by risk algorithms. Minimize inspection likelihood by: (1) Ensuring all documents are accurate and consistent; (2) Declaring correct HS codes (woven PP bags: 6305.33, woven PE bags: 6305.39); (3) Providing realistic valuations — under-declaration triggers penalties; (4) Including all required certificates (origin, phytosanitary, fumigation); (5) Using established freight forwarders with good customs relationships. If inspected, respond promptly to document requests. Delays in response escalate storage fees exponentially.
