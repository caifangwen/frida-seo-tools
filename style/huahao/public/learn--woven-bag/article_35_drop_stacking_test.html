01
Impact Testing

Drop Test Protocol (ISO 2206)

The drop test is the most widely recognized quality gate for woven bags. It simulates the impacts bags experience during manual handling, conveyor transfers, and accidental falls — providing a standardized measure of bag durability that buyers and suppliers can reference objectively.

Height, Orientation, and Number of Drops

Standard protocol (ISO 2206):

  • Test sample: 3–5 bags filled with actual product or standardized substitute (sand, gravel) to rated capacity
  • Drop height: Typically 1.0–1.5 meters depending on bag weight and application; 1.2 m is the most common specification for 25–50 kg bags
  • Impact surface: Rigid, horizontal concrete or steel surface without cushioning
  • Orientations: Flat (bottom impact), edge (side seam), and corner (diagonal impact)
  • Number of drops: One drop per bag per orientation; 3 bags × 3 orientations = 9 total drops minimum

Drop height by application:

Bag Weight Standard Drop Height Heavy-Duty Height
5–10 kg 0.8–1.0 m 1.2 m
10–25 kg 1.0–1.2 m 1.5 m
25–50 kg 1.2 m 1.5–1.8 m
50+ kg 1.2–1.5 m 1.8 m

Standard Note: ISO 2206 is the general transport packaging drop test standard. Industry-specific standards include: ASTM D5276 (US), ISTA 3A (parcel delivery simulation), and various cement industry specifications that may require 1.5 m drops. Always specify which standard you require in your RFQ.

02
Pass/Fail

Pass/Fail Criteria

Drop test results are evaluated against objective criteria that determine whether a bag design meets quality standards for its intended application. Understanding these criteria prevents disputes between buyers and suppliers.

Seam Failure, Tearing, and Contents Spillage

Critical failure (automatic reject):

  • Contents spillage: Any loss of product from the bag
  • Catastrophic rupture: Tear >5 cm in bag body or seam
  • Complete seam separation: Bottom or side seam opens fully
  • Handle detachment: Handle separates from bag body

Minor defects (conditional accept):

  • Small seam opening: <2 cm gap without spillage
  • Surface abrasion: Scuffing or fuzzing without structural damage
  • Minor elongation: Bag deformation that does not affect integrity
  • Single thread break: In chain-stitch seams without unraveling

Industry pass rates: Most buyers require 100% pass (zero critical failures) across all tested samples. Some commodity applications accept 90% pass rates (1 failure in 10 samples) for economy-grade bags. Premium and export applications universally require 100% pass.

03
Compression

Stacking Compression Test

While drop tests simulate impact, stacking compression tests simulate the sustained loads that palletized bags experience in warehouses and transport containers. This test is critical for products stored in multi-layer stacks.

Static Load for Warehouse Simulation

Test method: A filled bag is placed on a rigid surface and loaded with a calculated weight that simulates the bags stacked above it. The load is maintained for 24 hours, after which the bag is inspected for deformation, seam failure, and contents compaction.

Load calculation: Standard test load = (Number of bags above in stack) × (Average bag weight) × (Safety factor). Typical configurations:

  • Warehouse stack (8 layers): Test load = 7 bags above × 25 kg × 1.5 = 262.5 kg
  • Container stack (10 layers): Test load = 9 bags × 25 kg × 1.5 = 337.5 kg
  • Extended storage (12 layers): Test load = 11 bags × 25 kg × 1.5 = 412.5 kg

The 1.5 safety factor accounts for dynamic loads during transport (vibration, cornering, braking) that add to static stack weight.

Pass criteria: Bag must support the test load for 24 hours without: seam failure, fabric rupture, excessive deformation (>15% height reduction), or contents leakage.

📦
Real-World Correlation: Laboratory stacking tests use uniform, centered loads. Real-world stacks have uneven weight distribution, misaligned bags, and vibration. A bag that passes lab testing at 8-layer equivalent may fail in actual 6-layer transport. Build 20–30% safety margin into your stacking specifications.
04
Validation

Field Correlation

Laboratory tests are controlled and repeatable, but they cannot perfectly replicate the chaotic conditions of real-world logistics. Understanding the relationship between lab results and field performance helps set appropriate specifications.

Lab Results vs Real-World Performance

Drop test correlation: Bags that pass 1.2 m laboratory drops typically survive 0.8–1.0 m real-world falls. The difference comes from: uneven impact surfaces (gravel, pallets), angular impacts (not flat/edge/corner), pre-existing micro-damage from handling, and temperature effects. Cold weather makes PP more brittle, reducing real-world impact resistance by 20–40%.

Stacking correlation: Lab stacking tests overestimate real-world performance because they assume perfect alignment and uniform load. In practice, misaligned pallets, bag deformation, and vibration reduce effective stack capacity. Field experience suggests that safe real-world stack heights are 70–80% of laboratory-tested limits.

Aging effects: Laboratory tests use fresh bags. Bags stored in warehouses for 3–6 months before use may have degraded due to UV exposure, oxidation, or creep. For products with long distribution cycles, test aged samples (30 days at 40°C accelerates aging) rather than fresh production.

05
Quality Systems

Quality Assurance Integration

Testing is only valuable when integrated into a systematic quality management program. Random, one-off tests catch problems after they've occurred. Structured testing prevents problems from reaching customers.

In-Line and Batch Testing Schedules

Incoming material testing: Verify resin quality, tape strength, and fabric specifications before conversion begins. Test every batch of extruded tape for tensile strength and elongation. Reject off-spec material before it becomes finished bags.

In-process testing: During conversion, perform spot checks at regular intervals: seam strength every 2 hours, print quality every 500 bags, dimensions every 1,000 bags. In-process catches problems early, minimizing scrap.

Final inspection: Before shipment, test representative samples from each production lot. Typical sampling: 5 bags per 10,000 (AQL 1.0, General Inspection Level II). Test drop, seam strength, and print adhesion. Retain samples for 6–12 months in case of customer complaints.

Certificate of compliance: For export orders, provide test reports with shipment documentation. Include: test date, sample identification, test conditions, results, and inspector signature. This documentation is often required for customs clearance and customer acceptance.

  • Incoming: Tape tensile, fabric GSM, density verification
  • In-process: Seam strength, print quality, dimensions
  • Final: Drop test, compression, visual inspection
  • Retention: Samples retained 6–12 months
  • Documentation: CoC with every export shipment

Pro Tip: Require your supplier to perform drop tests using your actual product or a close substitute, not just sand. Product density, particle shape, and flow characteristics affect bag behavior during impact. A bag that passes with sand may fail with sharp gravel or fine powder. Specify test media in your quality agreement.

06
FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I perform drop tests myself?

Yes — a basic drop test requires no specialized equipment beyond a measuring tape and a hard surface. Mark the drop height on a wall, fill the bag to rated capacity, and release it (don't throw) so it falls freely. Evaluate for spillage and structural damage. While informal tests lack the precision of laboratory protocols, they are sufficient for spot-checking received goods. For dispute resolution or certification, use an accredited third-party laboratory with calibrated equipment and documented procedures.

How often should testing be performed?

At minimum, test every production batch (typically defined as one shift or one loom setup, whichever is smaller). For high-volume, stable production, statistical sampling (AQL-based) is acceptable. For new designs, new suppliers, or after process changes, test 100% of the first production run. For ongoing production, test frequency can reduce to 1 per 5,000–10,000 bags if historical quality is consistently good. Increase testing if complaint rates rise or specifications change.

What is the difference between drop test and impact test?

A drop test releases a filled bag from a specified height onto a hard surface — it tests the complete bag system (fabric, seams, closure) under realistic handling conditions. An impact test (like ASTM D1709 dart drop) uses a weighted dart or pendulum to strike flat fabric samples — it tests only fabric puncture resistance, not seam integrity. Both have value: dart impact predicts puncture from sharp objects; drop test predicts overall bag survival during handling. For woven bags, drop testing is more relevant to real-world performance.

Should I test empty or filled bags?

Always test filled bags. Empty bags have no internal pressure and deform differently than filled bags during impact. The product inside provides rigidity and distributes impact forces. Testing empty bags produces misleadingly good results — a flimsy empty bag may look acceptable but fail catastrophically when filled. Use actual product or standardized substitutes (sand for granular, concrete mix for cement testing) at rated fill weight.
