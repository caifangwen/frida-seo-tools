8×8 to 14×14 Explained

01
Fundamentals

What Do the Numbers Mean?

The density designation "8×8," "10×10," and so on describes the number of warp and weft tapes per unit length in the woven fabric. The first number indicates warp threads (running lengthwise, in the machine direction), and the second number indicates weft threads (running crosswise, in the transverse direction). These numbers are typically expressed per inch in imperial markets and per 10 centimeters in metric markets — though the ratios remain proportional.

Warp × Weft Threads Per Inch / CM

For example, a 10×10 woven fabric contains 10 warp tapes and 10 weft tapes per inch (or per 10 cm, depending on regional convention). This creates a balanced weave with equal thread count in both directions. Some specifications use unbalanced counts like 10×12, where the warp is slightly denser than the weft, but balanced weaves (equal warp and weft) are standard for woven bags.

The density number is independent of tape width and thickness. Two 10×10 fabrics can have different weights (GSM) if one uses thicker tapes than the other. However, for a given tape specification, higher density always means heavier fabric, greater strength, and better printability — but also higher material cost.

Quick Fact: "Thread count" in woven bag terminology refers to tape count, not textile yarn count. Woven bag tapes are flat plastic strips 2–4 mm wide, not round threads. A 10×10 bag fabric has 100 tape intersections per square inch, creating the characteristic grid-like structure.

02
Economy Grade

8×8: Economy Grade

The 8×8 density represents the lightest standard construction for woven bags. With only 8 tapes per inch in each direction, the fabric has visible open spaces between tapes, creating a loose, breathable weave that minimizes material usage.

Light Duty, Cost-Focused

8×8 fabric is typically paired with lower tape weights (denier) to produce fabrics in the 50–65 GSM range. This specification prioritizes cost over durability and is suitable for applications where bags experience minimal handling stress and short transport distances.

  • Typical GSM: 50–65 GSM
  • Tensile strength: 600–900 N (warp direction)
  • Load capacity: 5–10 kg recommended
  • Print quality: Limited — coarse weave creates uneven print surface
  • Common uses: Onions, potatoes, firewood, light grains, promotional bags, shopping totes
  • Cost position: Lowest per-bag cost in woven bag spectrum

Warning: Do not use 8×8 bags for products exceeding 10 kg or for export shipping. The loose weave has insufficient seam strength and abrasion resistance for heavy loads or extended handling. Breakage rates in 8×8 bags jump dramatically above 12 kg loads.

03
Standard Grade

10×10: Standard Grade

10×10 is the most widely specified woven bag density globally, accounting for approximately 60% of all woven bag production. It strikes the optimal balance between cost, strength, and printability for the majority of commercial applications.

Most Common Specification

The 10×10 density provides enough tape coverage to create a reasonably smooth printing surface while maintaining sufficient open structure for breathability. When combined with standard tape deniers, it produces 65–80 GSM fabric suitable for the industry's most common load range: 15–25 kg.

  • Typical GSM: 65–80 GSM
  • Tensile strength: 900–1,500 N (warp direction)
  • Load capacity: 10–25 kg
  • Print quality: Good — accepts 1–3 color flexographic printing with acceptable registration
  • Common uses: Rice, flour, sugar, animal feed, fertilizer (indoor), grains, seeds
  • Cost position: Best value — industry sweet spot
⭐
Industry Standard: When buyers don't specify density, most manufacturers default to 10×10 at 75 GSM. This specification handles 85% of all woven bag applications without issue. If you're uncertain about your requirements, start with 10×10 and test before customizing.
04
Heavy Duty

12×12: Heavy Duty

When applications demand greater strength, better print resolution, or extended outdoor durability, 12×12 density provides a significant step up from standard 10×10. The additional tapes per inch create a tighter, more uniform surface that supports premium branding and heavier loads.

Better Strength and Print Quality

The 12×12 weave's tighter structure distributes point loads across more tapes, reducing stress concentration at any single intersection. This translates to better puncture resistance, improved seam efficiency, and more consistent performance under dynamic loads (drops, throws, conveyor handling).

From a branding perspective, 12×12 fabric provides the smoothness needed for multi-color flexographic printing and the base layer quality required for BOPP lamination. Print registration improves because the tighter weave reduces substrate movement during printing.

  • Typical GSM: 80–100 GSM
  • Tensile strength: 1,400–2,200 N (warp direction)
  • Load capacity: 25–40 kg
  • Print quality: Very good — supports up to 4-color flexo and full BOPP lamination
  • Common uses: Cement, chemicals, export rice, premium retail, heavy minerals, construction aggregates
  • Cost position: 15–25% premium over 10×10
05
Premium Grade

14×14: Premium Grade

14×14 represents the highest standard density for commodity woven bags. At 14 tapes per inch in both directions, the fabric approaches a near-solid surface with minimal open area between tapes. This density is reserved for the most demanding applications where failure is not an option.

Maximum Durability

14×14 fabric is typically produced at 100–130 GSM and is capable of handling 40–50 kg loads with safety margins suitable for export shipping and rough handling. The dense weave also provides excellent abrasion resistance, making it the choice for products with sharp edges or rough surfaces that would damage lighter fabrics.

  • Typical GSM: 100–130 GSM
  • Tensile strength: 2,000–3,000 N (warp direction)
  • Load capacity: 40–50+ kg
  • Print quality: Excellent — supports full-color gravure and premium BOPP lamination
  • Common uses: Heavy cement (50kg), industrial chemicals, export bulk, abrasive minerals, high-value retail
  • Cost position: 35–50% premium over 10×10

Pro Tip: Before specifying 14×14, verify that your product actually needs this level of strength. Many buyers over-specify density out of caution, adding 30–50% to material costs unnecessarily. A 12×12 at 100 GSM often handles 50 kg loads adequately. Request drop-test data at your target load before committing to 14×14.

06
Technical Correlation

Density vs Weight Relationship

Density and GSM (grams per square meter) are related but distinct properties. Density describes tape count; GSM describes fabric weight. Understanding their relationship helps you specify bags correctly and evaluate supplier quotes.

GSM Correlation Chart

For a given tape specification, doubling density approximately doubles GSM. However, manufacturers can vary tape thickness (denier) independently of density, creating overlap ranges. A heavy 10×10 (with thick tapes) can weigh more than a light 12×12 (with thin tapes).

Density Typical GSM Range Tape Denier Range Load Capacity Best Application
8×8 50–65 GSM 600–800 5–10 kg Economy
10×10 65–80 GSM 750–1,000 10–25 kg Standard
12×12 80–100 GSM 900–1,200 25–40 kg Heavy Duty
14×14 100–130 GSM 1,000–1,400 40–50+ kg Premium

Practical specification tip: Always specify both density and GSM in your purchase orders. Specifying density alone leaves tape thickness open to interpretation; specifying GSM alone allows suppliers to use denser weaves with thinner tapes (which may have lower abrasion resistance). The combination of density + GSM locks in the construction you need.

07
FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get custom densities like 9×9 or 11×11?

Technically possible but rarely economical. Looms are set up with fixed reed counts and heddle configurations optimized for standard densities. Custom densities require reed changes that add setup costs and reduce production efficiency. Most manufacturers will quote 20–30% premiums for non-standard densities. If you need strength between 10×10 and 12×12, it's usually more cost-effective to specify 10×10 at higher GSM rather than 11×11.

Does higher density mean less breathability?

Yes. As density increases, the open area between tapes decreases, reducing air permeability. An 8×8 weave might allow 2.0 L/m²/s airflow, while 14×14 restricts this to under 0.3 L/m²/s. For products requiring ventilation (onions, potatoes, seeds), don't over-specify density. If you need both high strength and breathability, specify 10×10 with thicker tapes (higher denier) rather than jumping to 12×12.

Is density the same as "mesh count"?

Essentially yes, though "mesh count" is more commonly used in filtration and screen applications, while "density" or "thread count" is standard in woven bag terminology. Both refer to the number of threads/tapes per unit length. Some suppliers may use "ends per inch" (EPI) for warp and "picks per inch" (PPI) for weft — so 10×10 would be 10 EPI × 10 PPI.

How do I verify density in received goods?

Use a pick counter or magnifying glass with a 1-inch graticule (reticle). Lay the fabric flat, align the graticule with the tape direction, and count visible tapes across one inch. Count warp and weft separately. Acceptable tolerance is typically ±1 tape per inch per direction. For example, a spec of 10×10 should measure 9–11 × 9–11. Measure at least three locations per bag and average the results.
