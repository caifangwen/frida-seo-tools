Raw Materials

Raw Material (PP/PE Granules)

Polypropylene or polyethylene resin is the single largest cost component in woven bag production, typically representing 55–65% of the final bag price. Understanding resin pricing dynamics is essential for both buyers and suppliers.

Price Index Linkage and Volatility

PP and PE prices are commodity products traded on global markets with pricing referenced to regional indices:

  • Asia: CFR China or Southeast Asia spot prices, tracked by ICIS, Platts
  • Europe: FD Northwest Europe contract and spot prices
  • Americas: US Gulf Coast export prices, domestic contract prices
  • Middle East: FOB Middle East export prices (lowest global cost base due to feedstock advantage)

Resin prices correlate with crude oil and natural gas prices (the feedstocks for propylene and ethylene production) but also respond to regional supply-demand balances, refinery maintenance schedules, and new plant start-ups. In 2021–2022, COVID-related supply disruptions caused PP prices to spike 60–80% above historical averages.

Typical resin pricing (2024–2026 range):

📊
Price Protection: For large orders, consider resin price indexing clauses in contracts. Typical structures: fixed price for 30 days, or price adjusted monthly based on ICIS/Platts average +/- a conversion margin. This shares volatility risk between buyer and supplier.
02
Production

Extrusion and Tape Production

After resin purchase, the first manufacturing step is extrusion — melting polymer and forming it into flat tapes. This step accounts for approximately 15–20% of total bag cost and is where energy and labor first enter the cost model.

Energy and Labor per kg

Energy consumption: Extrusion is energy-intensive. Typical consumption is 0.25–0.40 kWh per kg of tape produced, depending on extruder efficiency and cooling requirements. At industrial electricity rates of $0.08–0.15/kWh, energy cost is $0.02–0.06 per kg of tape.

Labor: Extrusion lines require 2–4 operators per shift for monitoring, reel changes, and quality checks. In Asian manufacturing regions, extrusion labor adds $0.01–0.03 per kg. Automated lines with centralized control reduce labor but increase capital costs.

Yield loss: Startup scrap, edge trim, and off-spec tape typically account for 3–8% material loss. This "extrusion yield" directly affects cost — every percentage point of yield improvement drops per-kg tape cost. Best-practice facilities achieve 95%+ yield; older or less controlled lines may be at 90–92%.

  • Electricity: 0.25–0.40 kWh/kg ($0.02–0.06/kg)
  • Labor: $0.01–0.03/kg (varies by region)
  • Depreciation: $0.01–0.02/kg (extruder capital recovery)
  • Maintenance: $0.005–0.01/kg (dies, filters, wear parts)
  • Yield loss: 3–8% of resin input
03
Weaving

Weaving Cost

The weaving stage converts flat tapes into tubular or flat fabric. Weaving represents 10–15% of total bag cost and is primarily labor and capital-dependent.

Loom Speed and Efficiency

Circular looms: The most common equipment for woven bag production. Modern circular looms operate at 120–180 rpm (picks per minute), producing 1.5–3.0 meters of tubular fabric per minute depending on width and density. A single loom produces approximately 4,000–8,000 meters per 24-hour day.

Labor requirement: One operator typically monitors 4–8 circular looms, performing warp beam changes, quality checks, and minor adjustments. Labor cost per meter of fabric is $0.005–0.015 depending on region and automation level.

Efficiency factors: Loom efficiency (actual running time vs. scheduled time) typically ranges from 85–95%. Downtime causes include warp breaks, weft breaks, beam changes, and quality adjustments. Higher efficiency directly reduces per-meter weaving cost.

04
Finishing

Conversion (Cutting, Sewing, Printing)

Conversion transforms woven fabric into finished bags. This stage — cutting to length, sewing or heat-sealing, and printing — adds 15–25% to total cost depending on bag complexity and print requirements.

Labor-Intensive Steps

Cutting: Tubular fabric is cut to bag length using hot or cold knives. Hot cutting fuses edges to prevent fraying. Automated cutting lines process 30–60 bags per minute. Labor: minimal for automated lines; significant for manual cutting tables.

Sewing: The most labor-intensive conversion step. Manual sewing stations produce 10–20 bags per minute; automated sewing lines reach 25–40 bags per minute. Each bag requires bottom closure and often top hem or full closure. Thread cost is minimal ($0.001–0.003 per bag).

Printing: Flexographic printing adds $0.02–0.08 per bag depending on color count and coverage. Setup waste (50–200 meters) is amortized across the run length — short runs have higher per-bag print costs. BOPP lamination with gravure printing adds $0.15–0.40 per bag.

Additional features: Handles (+$0.02–0.10), gussets (+$0.01–0.03), liners (+$0.05–0.15), valves (+$0.03–0.08), and anti-slip coating (+$0.01–0.03) each add incremental cost.

Value Addition

Lamination (If Applicable)

BOPP or PE lamination adds significant cost but also significant value through moisture protection, print quality, and brand differentiation. Understanding lamination cost structure helps buyers evaluate the ROI of upgrading from plain to laminated bags.

Film, Printing, and Bonding

BOPP film: 20–25 micron transparent film costs $0.08–0.15 per bag depending on bag size and film thickness. Pearlized, matte, or metallized films cost 20–40% more.

Gravure printing: Cylinder engraving is a fixed cost ($300–800 per color). Ink and printing operations add $0.03–0.08 per color per bag. A 4-color design with full coverage adds $0.20–0.40 per bag in printing costs.

Adhesive lamination: Solvent-based, water-based, or solvent-free adhesives bond the printed film to woven fabric. Adhesive and lamination labor add $0.02–0.05 per bag. Solvent-free adhesives cost more but are preferred for food-contact applications.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do quotes vary so much between suppliers?

Price variation comes from: (1) Resin sourcing — some suppliers buy spot market, others have long-term contracts; (2) Equipment efficiency — modern automated lines have lower conversion costs than older manual facilities; (3) Scale — large producers achieve economies of scale impossible for small workshops; (4) Quality standards — some suppliers cut corners on GSM, film thickness, or ink quality; (5) Overhead structure — integrated manufacturers (own extrusion, weaving, and conversion) have cost advantages over trading companies that subcontract. Always request detailed breakdowns to identify the source of price differences.

How much should I expect prices to change month-to-month?

In stable markets, woven bag prices change 1–3% monthly, tracking resin price movements. During volatile periods (2021–2022 energy crisis, supply chain disruptions), prices can swing 10–20% in a single month. For budget planning, assume ±15% annual price variation around a baseline. For contract negotiations, resin indexation clauses protect both parties from extreme volatility while allowing fair pricing in stable periods.

Can I get accurate costing without revealing my target price?

Yes — request "open book" or "cost-plus" quotations where the supplier breaks down resin, conversion, and margin separately. Most legitimate suppliers will provide this for serious buyers. Be prepared to share your estimated annual volume and specifications. If a supplier refuses to provide any cost breakdown and insists on a single lump-sum price, this may indicate they are a trading company (not manufacturer) or they are pricing opportunistically. Detailed RFQs with full specifications yield more transparent pricing.

What is the typical supplier profit margin?

Supplier margins in woven bag manufacturing typically range from 5–12% depending on market conditions, order size, and relationship length. Commodity plain bags in competitive markets may see 3–5% margins. Premium laminated bags with complex printing can support 10–15% margins. Trading companies (buying from manufacturers and reselling) typically add 8–15% margin on top of factory prices. Understanding these margin ranges helps evaluate whether a quote is reasonable or inflated.
