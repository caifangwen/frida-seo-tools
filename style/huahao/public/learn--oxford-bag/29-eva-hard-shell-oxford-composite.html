02
Manufacturing

EVA Molding Process

Ethylene-vinyl acetate foam is molded into shape using heat and pressure, creating a semi-rigid shell that defines the bag's structure and protection level.

EVA Grades

Mid-density EVA at 150–200 kg/m³ is the standard for composite bag shells. It provides sufficient rigidity for protection while remaining light enough for portable bags. Low-density EVA at 80–120 kg/m³ is used for lightweight padding and inserts. High-density and cross-linked EVA at 220–300 kg/m³ offer maximum protection for premium cases.

Molding Methods

Compression molding places EVA foam sheet in a heated mold at 150–180°C with 5–20 tons of pressure, ideal for simple shapes and high volume. Thermal vacuum forming heats the sheet until pliable and draws it over a mold with vacuum suction, suited for complex curves and medium volume. CNC machining carves EVA blocks for prototypes and complex internal geometries.

Mold Design

Aluminum molds cost $3,000–$8,000 with 3–4 week lead times for high-volume precision. Steel molds at $5,000–$15,000 last longest for very high volume. Silicone or resin molds at $500–$1,500 enable rapid prototyping and low-volume production. Mold cost is the primary barrier to entry for EVA composite bags.

03
Materials

Oxford Overlay

The oxford fabric skin provides the exterior aesthetic, abrasion resistance, and branding surface.

Lamination Methods

Flame lamination is the most common method for EVA plus oxford composites. A thin layer of EVA on the fabric back is melted and pressed onto the molded shell, creating a permanent bond without additional adhesives. Adhesive and hot-melt lamination offer good to excellent durability at moderate cost. Sewn overlay provides a repairable, traditional construction at higher labor cost.

Fabric Selection

Camera bags typically use 600D–900D polyester oxford for durability and professional printability. Tool cases prefer 900D–1680D nylon oxford for abrasion resistance. Instrument bags use 420D–600D oxford with Jacquard patterns for premium aesthetics. The oxford layer is typically 0.3–0.6mm thick.

04
Markets

Applications

EVA + oxford composites excel where moderate-to-high protection is needed in a portable format.

Camera equipment is valuable, fragile, and sensitive to impact. EVA composite camera bags offer rigidity that protects lenses and bodies from crushing, molded dividers that hold equipment securely, and lighter weight than aluminum or plastic hard cases. Typical specifications use 200 kg/m³ EVA shells with 600D oxford overlay and 10mm PE foam interior lining.

Tool cases benefit from shape retention that prevents collapse when open, custom-fit molded pockets for specific tools, and rigid forms that allow secure stacking. Instrument and electronics bags require shock absorption and vibration damping for sensitive components.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Can EVA shells be customized for specific products?

Yes, but mold cost is required. Custom molded interiors that precisely fit specific cameras, tools, or devices offer maximum protection and premium positioning. CNC-cut foam inserts are a lower-cost alternative for low volumes.

Are EVA composite bags waterproof?

The EVA shell itself is waterproof, but seams where the oxford overlay meets zippers or hardware can leak. For waterproof requirements, specify seam sealing, waterproof zippers, and gasketed closures.

What is the minimum order for EVA composite bags?

Most manufacturers require 500–1,000 units minimum to justify mold investment. Below this threshold, consider sewn foam-padded bags or universal foam insert systems.

How do EVA composites compare to polypropylene hard cases?

EVA + oxford is lighter and more aesthetically flexible than PP hard cases but offers less impact resistance. PP cases are preferred for shipping and extreme conditions; EVA composites are better for daily transport and professional appearance.
