Certification Standards

What Is GRS Certification?

The Global Recycled Standard (GRS) is an internationally recognized, voluntary standard developed by Textile Exchange—a global nonprofit organization dedicated to accelerating sustainable textile production. GRS verifies recycled content in products and tracks it through the entire supply chain, from recycler to final product, ensuring transparency and credibility for brands making sustainability claims.

For B2B buyers and brand managers, understanding GRS certification is critical because it serves as the foundation for legitimate environmental marketing. Without GRS certification, claims like "made from recycled materials" lack independent verification and may be viewed skeptically by consumers and regulators alike. The standard has gained particular importance in the EU market, where regulatory scrutiny of environmental claims has intensified under the Green Claims Directive.

GRS certification goes beyond simply verifying recycled content. It encompasses a comprehensive set of environmental and social criteria that manufacturers must meet throughout the production process. This includes restrictions on hazardous chemicals, requirements for wastewater treatment, fair labor practices, and safe working conditions. For brands committed to genuine sustainability—not just greenwashing—GRS certification provides a credible, independently verified framework.

Manufacturing Process

From Bottle to Fabric: How Recycled Oxford Fabric Is Made

The transformation of post-consumer PET bottles into high-performance oxford fabric is a remarkable feat of modern textile engineering. The process involves multiple stages, each carefully controlled to ensure the final fabric meets stringent quality and sustainability standards. Understanding this process helps B2B buyers appreciate the value proposition of recycled materials and communicate it effectively to end consumers.

The journey begins with collection and sorting programs that gather PET bottles from recycling streams. These bottles—typically water bottles, soda bottles, and food containers—are sorted by color and quality to ensure consistency in the final product. Clear and light-colored bottles are preferred for lighter fabric shades, while darker bottles are used for black and navy fabrics. This initial sorting stage is critical because contamination or inconsistent raw materials can compromise the entire production batch.

1
Collection & Sorting

Post-consumer PET bottles are collected from recycling programs and sorted by color and quality. Clear and light-colored bottles are preferred for lighter fabric shades.

2
Cleaning & Shredding

Bottles are thoroughly cleaned, labels and caps removed, then shredded into small flakes. This stage ensures purity of the recycled material.

3
Melting & Pelletizing

Clean flakes are melted and extruded into small pellets called rPET (recycled PET) pellets—the raw material for fiber production.

4
Fiber Extrusion

Pellets are melted again and extruded through spinnerets to create polyester filaments of specified denier (210D-1680D).

5
Weaving

Filaments are woven into oxford fabric using the standard basket-weave pattern, creating the characteristic texture and structure.

6
Dyeing & Finishing

Fabric is dyed using eco-friendly dyes and coated with PU or PVC coating (eco-certified options available for sustainable brands).

7
GRS Certification

The entire process is audited by independent certifiers (Control Union, ICEA, etc.) to verify recycled content and chain of custody.

FAQ

FAQ: GRS Recycled Oxford Fabric

Is recycled oxford fabric as good as virgin?

Yes! Physical properties are within 3% of virgin fabric—a negligible difference that doesn't affect real-world performance. Most users cannot tell the difference. Tensile strength, tear resistance, abrasion resistance, and color fastness all match virgin fabric within normal manufacturing tolerances.

How much more does GRS recycled fabric cost?

Currently 10-15% more than virgin polyester. However, the price gap is narrowing each year as production scales up—from +33% in 2022 to +16% in 2026. The trend suggests further narrowing as recycled material adoption increases globally.

Can recycled oxford fabric be any color?

Standard colors (black, navy, grey, royal blue, red, green) are widely available. Custom colors are possible but require higher MOQs. The color range is growing but still more limited than virgin polyester due to the sorting and processing of recycled PET bottles.

Is GRS certification required for recycled fabric?

Not legally required, but essential for credible marketing. Without GRS certification, you cannot make verified recycled content claims. For B2B buyers targeting eco-conscious consumers and EU/US markets, GRS certification is effectively mandatory.

Can I get GRS fabric with PVC coating?

Technically yes, but PVC is not eco-friendly. For truly sustainable products, use water-based PU coating. PVC coating contradicts the sustainability message and is not recommended for brands marketing to eco-conscious consumers.

How many bottles are in a bag?

Approximately: Shopping tote: 8-10 bottles, Backpack: 15-20 bottles, Travel duffel: 25-30 bottles. These numbers vary based on bag size and fabric weight, but provide a useful marketing message for end consumers.

Can I request samples?

Yes, always request samples before placing orders. Reputable suppliers provide free samples of GRS certified fabric with supporting documentation. This allows you to verify quality and confirm the material meets your specifications before committing to production.
