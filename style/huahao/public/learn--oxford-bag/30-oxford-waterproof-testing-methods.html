Oxford Waterproof Testing Methods: AATCC & ISO Standards Guide

How to test and specify waterproof performance for oxford fabrics and bags—from spray ratings and hydrostatic head to immersion testing and real-world interpretation.

02
Surface Repellency

Spray Rating Test (AATCC 22)

The spray rating test evaluates how well water beads up and rolls off a fabric surface rather than wetting it out. It is the standard method for assessing DWR treatment effectiveness.

The test mounts a fabric specimen at a 45-degree angle, sprays 250ml of water from a standardized nozzle at 150mm distance, taps the specimen gently to dislodge loose water, and compares the wetting pattern against standard photographs to assign a rating from 0 to 100.

Rating Visual Description Performance
100 No sticking or wetting Excellent repellency
90 Slight random sticking Very good
80 Wetting at spray points Good
70 Partial wetting Moderate
50 Complete wetting of upper surface Poor
0 Complete wetting of both sides None
Table 2: AATCC 22 spray rating scale

A rating of 80 or above indicates effective DWR. However, spray rating does not measure waterproofing—a fabric can score 100 but leak under pressure if the coating or membrane is compromised.

03
Pressure Resistance

Hydrostatic Head Test (AATCC 127 / ISO 811)

The hydrostatic head test is the definitive measurement of fabric waterproofness. It determines the water pressure, expressed as height of water column in millimeters, that causes leakage through the fabric.

The test clamps a fabric specimen over a water-filled chamber and increases pressure at a controlled rate of 60 cm H₂O per minute. The pressure at which water first penetrates to the fabric's reverse side is recorded and reported in millimeters.

Hydrostatic Head Classification Suitable For
0–500mm Not waterproof Indoor use only
500–1,000mm Water-resistant Light drizzle, short exposure
1,000–3,000mm Waterproof (light) Normal rain, moderate exposure
3,000–5,000mm Waterproof (good) Heavy rain, extended exposure
5,000–10,000mm Waterproof (excellent) Severe weather, all-day use
10,000mm+ Fully waterproof Submersion, marine use
Table 3: Hydrostatic head interpretation

For most bag applications, 1,500–3,000mm is sufficient. Bags intended for heavy rain or marine use should specify 5,000mm or higher.

04
Simulated Conditions

Bundesmann Test (DIN 53888)

The Bundesmann test simulates wind-driven rain—conditions more severe than static spray tests.

The method mounts fabric over a cupped holder, sprays water upward onto the fabric underside to simulate wind pressure, rotates the specimen continuously during exposure, and measures water absorption after 10 minutes. Results are reported as grams of water absorbed per square meter.

Absorption below 1 g/m² indicates excellent performance with essentially no penetration. This test is particularly relevant for outdoor bags used in storm conditions where wind forces rain through fabric that would resist static spray.

05
Real-World Validation

Immersion Test

For complete bags rather than just fabric, immersion testing verifies real-world waterproof performance including seams, zippers, and closures.

The protocol places weighed absorbent material inside the bag, seals it using the normal closure method, submerges it to 0.5–1.0m depth for 15 minutes to 24 hours, then measures weight gain of the absorbent material. Any weight gain equals water ingress.

Immersion testing is the only method that evaluates the complete system. A fabric may pass hydrostatic head testing while the finished bag fails due to zipper leakage or seam penetration.

06
Procurement

Specifying Test Requirements

When sourcing oxford fabric or finished bags, include clear test specifications to prevent disputes and ensure consistent quality.

Recommended specifications include test standard such as AATCC 127 or ISO 811, minimum hydrostatic head of at least 3,000mm, spray rating of 80 or above if DWR treated, minimum 3 specimens per batch, testing frequency of every production lot or monthly, and third-party verification from an ISO 17025 accredited laboratory.

07
FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between waterproof and water-resistant?

Water-resistant fabrics resist light rain and splashes. Waterproof fabrics withstand sustained water pressure. The distinction is quantitative: less than 1,000mm hydrostatic head is water-resistant; 1,000mm or above is waterproof to varying degrees.

Can I test fabric samples myself?

Basic spray tests can be approximated with a spray bottle and standardized procedure, but hydrostatic head testing requires specialized equipment. For procurement decisions, always request third-party test reports from accredited laboratories.

Why do some waterproof bags leak at the seams?

Fabric waterproofing and seam waterproofing are separate. Even fully waterproof fabric will leak at sewn seams unless they are taped, welded, or sealed. Always specify seam sealing for bags marketed as waterproof.

How does washing affect waterproof test results?

DWR treatments degrade with washing, reducing spray ratings. PU and PVC coatings are more durable but can crack with repeated flexing. Specify post-wash testing for bags intended for long-term use.
