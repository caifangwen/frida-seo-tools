# Oxford Waterproof Testing Methods: AATCC & ISO Standards Guide

**Waterproof and water-resistant testing** provides the data B2B buyers need to verify supplier claims and specify appropriate fabric performance. For brand owners and procurement managers, understanding test methods prevents costly mismatches between marketed performance and real-world results.

This guide explains spray rating, hydrostatic head, Bundesmann, and immersion tests, with interpretation guidelines for oxford bag applications.

---

## Quick Answer: Test Methods at a Glance

| Test | Standard | Measures | Best For |
|------|----------|----------|----------|
| **Spray rating** | AATCC 22 / ISO 4920 | Surface water repellency | DWR effectiveness |
| **Hydrostatic head** | AATCC 127 / ISO 811 | Water pressure resistance | Waterproof rating |
| **Bundesmann** | DIN 53888 | Wind-driven rain simulation | Outdoor gear |
| **Immersion** | Internal protocols | Complete bag submersion | Dry bags, marine |

**Bottom Line**: Hydrostatic head (AATCC 127) is the most important test for oxford bag buyers. It directly quantifies how much water pressure fabric can withstand before leaking.

## Spray Rating Test (AATCC 22)

The spray rating test evaluates how well water beads up and rolls off a fabric surface rather than wetting it out.

### Test Method

1. Mount fabric specimen at 45° angle
2. Spray 250ml water from a standardized nozzle (150mm distance)
3. Tap specimen gently to dislodge loose water
4. Compare wetting pattern against standard photographs
5. Assign rating from 0 (complete wetting) to 100 (no wetting)

### Rating Scale

| Rating | Visual Description | Performance |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **100** | No sticking or wetting | Excellent repellency |
| **90** | Slight random sticking | Very good |
| **80** | Wetting at spray points | Good |
| **70** | Partial wetting | Moderate |
| **50** | Complete wetting of upper surface | Poor |
| **0** | Complete wetting of upper and lower surface | None |

### Application to Oxford Bags

Spray rating primarily tests the effectiveness of DWR (durable water repellent) treatment on the face fabric. A rating of 80+ indicates effective DWR; below 70 suggests retreatment is needed.

**Limitation**: Spray rating does not measure waterproofing. A fabric can score 100 on spray rating but leak under pressure if the coating or membrane is compromised.

## Hydrostatic Head Test (AATCC 127 / ISO 811)

The hydrostatic head test is the definitive measurement of fabric waterproofness. It determines the water pressure (expressed as height of water column in millimeters) that causes leakage through the fabric.

### Test Method

1. Clamp fabric specimen over a water-filled chamber
2. Increase water pressure at a controlled rate (60 cm H₂O/minute for AATCC 127)
3. Record pressure at which water first penetrates to the fabric's reverse side
4. Report result in millimeters (mm H₂O)

### Interpreting Results

| Hydrostatic Head | Classification | Suitable For |
|-----------------|----------------|--------------|
| **0–500mm** | Not waterproof | Indoor use only |
| **500–1,000mm** | Water-resistant | Light drizzle, short exposure |
| **1,000–3,000mm** | Waterproof (light) | Normal rain, moderate exposure |
| **3,000–5,000mm** | Waterproof (good) | Heavy rain, extended exposure |
| **5,000–10,000mm** | Waterproof (excellent) | Severe weather, all-day use |
| **10,000mm+** | Fully waterproof | Submersion, marine use |

### Oxford Fabric Benchmarks

| Fabric / Coating | Typical Hydrostatic Head |
|-----------------|-------------------------|
| Uncoated 600D oxford | 200–400mm |
| PU coated 30g/m² | 1,000–2,000mm |
| PU coated 60g/m² | 3,000–5,000mm |
| PVC coated | 5,000–10,000mm |
| TPU coated | 8,000–15,000mm |
| WPB membrane (3L) | 10,000–30,000mm |

For most bag applications, 1,500–3,000mm is sufficient. Bags intended for heavy rain or marine use should specify 5,000mm+.

## Bundesmann Test (DIN 53888)

The Bundesmann test simulates wind-driven rain—conditions more severe than static spray tests.

### Test Method

1. Mount fabric over a cupped holder
2. Spray water upward onto the fabric underside (simulating wind pressure)
3. Rotate specimen continuously during exposure
4. Measure water absorption after specified time (typically 10 minutes)
5. Report as grams of water absorbed per square meter

### Interpretation

| Absorption | Performance |
|-----------|-------------|
| **&lt;1 g/m²** | Excellent (essentially no penetration) |
| **1–5 g/m²** | Very good |
| **5–10 g/m²** | Good |
| **10–20 g/m²** | Moderate |
| **&gt;20 g/m²** | Poor |

The Bundesmann test is particularly relevant for outdoor bags used in storm conditions where wind forces rain through fabric that would resist static spray.

## Immersion Test

For complete bags (not just fabric), immersion testing verifies real-world waterproof performance.

### Test Protocol

1. Place absorbent material (weighed paper towels) inside the bag
2. Seal bag using normal closure method
3. Submerge in water tank to specified depth (typically 0.5–1.0m)
4. Hold for specified time (15 minutes to 24 hours)
5. Remove, dry exterior, open bag
6. Weigh absorbent material; weight gain = water ingress

### Pass Criteria

| Application | Maximum Allowable Ingress |
|------------|--------------------------|
| **General water-resistant bag** | &lt;1% of bag volume |
| **Dry bag (canoe/kayak)** | 0% (completely dry interior) |
| **Camera/electronics case** | 0% |
| **Medical transport** | 0% |

Immersion testing is the only method that evaluates seams, zippers, and closures—not just fabric. A fabric may pass hydrostatic head testing while the finished bag fails due to zipper leakage.

## FAQ: Waterproof Testing

### Q: What is the difference between waterproof and water-resistant?
**A:** Water-resistant fabrics resist light rain and splashes. Waterproof fabrics withstand sustained water pressure. The distinction is quantitative: &lt;1,000mm hydrostatic head = water-resistant; 1,000mm+ = waterproof (to varying degrees).

### Q: Can I test fabric samples myself?
**A:** Basic spray tests can be approximated with a spray bottle and standardized procedure, but hydrostatic head testing requires specialized equipment. For procurement decisions, always request third-party test reports from accredited laboratories.

### Q: Why do some "waterproof" bags leak at the seams?
**A:** Fabric waterproofing and seam waterproofing are separate. Even fully waterproof fabric will leak at sewn seams unless they are taped, welded, or sealed. Always specify seam sealing for bags marketed as waterproof.

### Q: How does washing affect waterproof test results?
**A:** DWR treatments degrade with washing, reducing spray ratings. PU and PVC coatings are more durable but can crack with repeated flexing. Specify post-wash testing (e.g., "≥3,000mm after 5 washes") for bags intended for long-term use.
