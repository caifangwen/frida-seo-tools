02
Material Science

What Is TPU?

Thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) is a class of polyurethane plastics that combine the mechanical properties of rubber with the processability of thermoplastics. Unlike conventional PU coatings that cure into a thermoset network, TPU remains thermoplastic—meaning it can be melted, reshaped, and recycled. This fundamental difference in polymer architecture gives TPU its unique combination of performance benefits.

The chemistry behind TPU involves a polyaddition reaction between diisocyanates (MDI or HDI), long-chain polyols (polyether or polyester based), and short-chain diols that act as chain extenders. This reaction creates a segmented polymer structure with alternating hard and soft segments, each contributing distinct properties to the final material.

Segment Type Function Properties
Hard segments Physical cross-links Strength, heat resistance
Soft segments Elasticity Flexibility, low-temp performance
Table 2: TPU segmented polymer structure

The ratio of hard to soft segments can be precisely adjusted during manufacturing to create TPUs ranging from soft and rubbery to hard and rigid. This gives bag manufacturers significant control over the final coating properties, allowing customization for specific applications from flexible dry bags to structured technical packs.

03
Performance

Advantages Over PVC

For bag manufacturers considering a switch from PVC to TPU, the performance benefits are substantial and span multiple dimensions from regulatory compliance to long-term durability.

No Plasticizers Required

PVC requires the addition of plasticizers—typically phthalates—to achieve the flexibility needed for bag applications. These plasticizers create several well-documented problems: they can migrate to the surface over time causing stickiness and surface contamination; they are increasingly restricted by REACH, CPSIA, and other global regulations; and they degrade in cold temperatures, making PVC brittle and prone to cracking.

TPU is inherently flexible due to its soft polymer segments. No plasticizers are needed, which means TPU-coated oxford maintains consistent performance across its entire service life without the regulatory baggage or performance degradation associated with plasticized PVC.

Superior Low-Temperature Performance

Temperature PVC Behavior TPU Behavior
+20°C Flexible Flexible
0°C Stiffening Flexible
-20°C Brittle, cracks Still flexible
-40°C Shatters Retains elasticity
Table 3: Low-temperature flexibility comparison

This dramatic difference in cold-weather performance makes TPU-coated bags ideal for outdoor winter gear, ski equipment bags, and cold-chain logistics applications where PVC would fail catastrophically.

Better Flexibility and Hand Feel

TPU-coated oxford fabric feels noticeably softer and more fabric-like than PVC-coated alternatives. Bags made with TPU drape better, pack more efficiently, and do not have the plastic rigidity that consumers often associate with lower-quality products. This improved tactile quality supports premium positioning and higher retail price points.

04
Sustainability

Environmental Profile

Sustainability is no longer optional for brands targeting EU and North American markets. TPU offers meaningful environmental advantages that can be leveraged in marketing, certification, and product positioning.

Recyclability

Because TPU is thermoplastic rather than thermoset, it can be re-melted and reprocessed into new products with minimal property loss. Mechanical recycling grinds scrap material into pellets that can be re-extruded, while chemical recycling breaks TPU back to its constituent raw materials. This contrasts sharply with cured PU and PVC, which are difficult to recycle and typically end up in landfills or incineration.

Lower VOC Emissions

During coating application, TPU emits significantly lower volatile organic compounds (VOCs) than PVC plastisols or solvent-based PU systems. This improves factory air quality, reduces worker exposure concerns, and simplifies compliance with increasingly strict emissions regulations in manufacturing regions.

Tear Strength

TPU's inherent elasticity allows it to absorb and distribute impact energy across the fabric surface, significantly improving tear propagation resistance. This is critical for bags that encounter sharp edges, rough handling, or heavy dynamic loads during transport and use.

Hydrolysis Resistance

One of PU coating's known weaknesses is hydrolysis—gradual chemical degradation when exposed to moisture and heat over extended periods. TPU offers superior hydrolysis resistance, particularly polyester-based TPU formulations, making it the preferred choice for bags used in humid climates or marine environments.

Manufacturing

TPU Coating Application Methods

TPU is applied to oxford fabric using two primary industrial methods, each suited to different production scales and performance requirements.

Extrusion Lamination

Molten TPU film is extruded directly onto the fabric surface and bonded under controlled heat and pressure. This continuous process produces a uniform, consistent coating thickness ideal for high-volume production runs. Extrusion lamination offers excellent adhesion and is the most common method for large-scale bag fabric production.

Hot-Melt Adhesive Lamination

Pre-formed TPU film is activated by heat and bonded to the fabric using a precision nip roller system. This method offers greater flexibility in TPU grade selection and is often preferred for smaller production runs or when using specialized TPU formulations with unique surface properties.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Is TPU coating fully biodegradable?

No—TPU is not biodegradable. However, it is recyclable and has a lower environmental impact than PVC across its full lifecycle. For biodegradable options, some manufacturers now offer bio-based TPUs made from renewable polyols derived from plant sources.

Can TPU-coated fabric be welded instead of sewn?

Yes. One of TPU's major advantages is its thermoplastic nature, which allows RF welding, hot-air welding, and ultrasonic welding. This creates seamless, fully waterproof joints ideal for dry bags, marine applications, and any product where stitch holes would compromise waterproof integrity.

Does TPU yellow over time?

Some TPU grades can yellow with prolonged UV exposure due to the aromatic diisocyanates used in standard formulations. For applications requiring long-term color stability, specify aliphatic TPU (using HDI instead of MDI), which has superior UV resistance but costs approximately 20–30% more.

What is the typical coating weight for TPU on oxford bags?

TPU coatings for bag applications typically range from 50g/m² to 150g/m², depending on the required waterproof rating and fabric base weight. 600D oxford usually uses 80–100g/m² TPU for the optimal balance of performance and hand feel.
